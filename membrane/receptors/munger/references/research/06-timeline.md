# Munger Timeline — Biographical Chronology with Intellectual Turning Points

The corpus is closed. Munger died Nov 28, 2023, age 99. The densest pure-Munger record is the 2014–2023 Daily Journal annual meetings era, when he was solo on stage without Buffett. This timeline foregrounds that late-life crystallization.

## Turning Points (the bend-points)

These are the moments where the way Munger thought changed — not just events, but rewrites of his operating system.

1. **1955 — Son Teddy's death from leukemia.** The Stoic baseline. Munger walks Pasadena streets weeping, then goes to work. Foundation of the iron prescription: envy, resentment, revenge, self-pity are perfectly disastrous modes of thought. Everything later builds on this floor.

2. **1959 — First dinner with Buffett at the Omaha Club.** Not an intellectual turning point — a *partnership* turning point. Buffett's mentor Graham had just retired; Munger's father had just died. Both men were available. Five minutes in, Munger was rolling on the floor laughing at his own jokes. Buffett: "I'm not going to find another guy like this."

3. **1972 — See's Candies.** The single biggest doctrine pivot in either man's life. Munger drags Buffett away from Graham cigar-butt deep-value and into "fair price for a great business." Buffett: he "hit me over the head with a two by four." Every subsequent Berkshire investment — Coca-Cola, Apple, BYD — descends from this trade.

4. **1980 — Lost left eye.** The personal application of the iron prescription. Botched cataract surgery, sympathetic ophthalmia risk, eye removed, learned Braille. Munger does not sue, does not blame. Stoicism is now operational, not theoretical. This is when "stand it" becomes the answer to almost everything.

5. **1994 — USC Marshall "Elementary Worldly Wisdom."** Thirty years of private latticework thinking crystallizes into a public doctrine. Mental models from multiple disciplines, ~80–90 of them, arrayed across psychology, economics, physics, biology, mathematics. After 1994 Munger is no longer just an investor — he's a public epistemologist.

6. **2008 — BYD.** Modification, not replacement, of the "no tech" rule. A single exceptional founder (Wang Chuanfu) plus a national tailwind (China's industrial scale, EV inflection) can override the heuristic. This is the late-life proof that Munger's framework was a latticework, not a checklist — rules bend when the underlying causal structure changes.

7. **2018+ — Crypto and EBITDA absolutism.** Not evolution — *crystallization*. Final-form Munger doesn't hedge. Bitcoin is "rat poison squared," "demented," "contrary to the interests of civilization." EBITDA earnings are "bullshit earnings." Late-life Munger sharpens, not softens. The 2014–2023 Daily Journal record is dense precisely because the filtering layer was gone.

## Final Crystallized State (2020–2023)

What were Munger's final mature positions on the major topics, as recorded in the Daily Journal meetings, the Acquired interview, and the late CNBC appearances? The 2014–2023 archive is consistent enough that one can read this as a settled doctrine.

**On investing.** Concentration over diversification ("diworsification"). A handful of great companies held forever beats a portfolio. Costco, BYD, Berkshire — model holdings. Index funds are fine for people who don't know what they're doing. "Sit on your ass investing" — most action destroys value.

**On China.** Bullish on Chinese companies despite political risk. BYD is "the best investment we ever made at Berkshire." Alibaba was "one of the worst mistakes I ever made" — overestimated Jack Ma, underestimated the regulatory risk. China has better engineers and a more competent government than the U.S. on infrastructure execution.

**On crypto.** Absolute contempt. Stupid, evil, contrary to civilization. Should have been banned. Comparable to a venereal disease. No nuance, no concession.

**On EBITDA.** "Bullshit earnings." Real earnings net of capex and stock-based compensation. Wall Street lies with adjusted metrics.

**On stock-based compensation.** A real cost. Companies that pretend otherwise are running fraud-adjacent accounting.

**On academia.** Soft sciences — especially economics as taught — are too narrow. Behavioral finance vindicated his 1995 talk; economics departments still missed it. Business schools teach EMH and CAPM despite the evidence.

**On envy.** "It's the one of the seven deadly sins that's not even fun." Avoid envious people; do not be one.

**On Stoicism (his version).** The iron prescription: avoid envy, resentment, revenge, self-pity. "You can't really live a successful life unless you can stand it." Every misfortune becomes an opportunity to behave well.

**On longevity.** Eat well, stay curious, avoid disastrous decisions, pick good partners, read constantly, sit with smart people. He attributed his late-life sharpness to "having something to look forward to" and to never having retired.

**On Buffett.** Their partnership was the single most important relationship of his professional life. He repeatedly said Buffett was the better investor. Buffett, repeatedly, said Munger was the architect of modern Berkshire.

**On his own legacy.** He wanted to be remembered for the partnership, the mental-models framework, and Daily Journal — not the Munger Hall fight. He acknowledged the dorm criticism but never retreated from the design.
