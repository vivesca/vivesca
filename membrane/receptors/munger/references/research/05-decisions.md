# Munger — Decisions

> Distillation Agent 5 of 6. Dimension: actual major decisions, including reversals, regrets, and anti-decisions. Decisions are higher signal than statements — where the espoused framework breaks, the truth shows.

## 1. Career decisions

### 1.1 1962 — Closed law practice to focus on investing

- **Year:** 1962
- **Context:** 38 years old. Successful Los Angeles lawyer. Father had died; trip back to Omaha to administer the estate brought him into deeper contact with Buffett. Already running side investments; legal income was the floor, not the ceiling.
- **Decision:** Co-founded the law firm Munger, Tolles & Hills in February 1962 (with four colleagues from Musick, Peeler & Garrett), but in parallel set up an investment partnership with Jack Wheeler — Wheeler, Munger & Co. The law firm was simultaneously launched and exited from; the practice was a "backstop rather than an ending objective."
- **Stated reasoning:** Saw legal career as a means to an end — a way to fund his growing interest in business and investing. Had observed wealthy clients and decided he wanted to be on that side of the table. Buffett had explicitly told him the law was a fine hobby but a bad business.
- **Outcome:** Wheeler, Munger ran 1962–1975 with strong returns through the 1960s before the 1973–74 crash.
- **Retrospective reflection:** Munger has said the law firm was the best decision and the partnership the most lucrative; he kept his name on the law firm out of vanity and identity, not income.
- **Framework consistency: HIGH.** Matches espoused doctrine of "deserve what you want" and concentrating on highest-leverage activity.
- Sources: [Charlie Munger – Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger); [The Motley Fool – Charlie Munger, Half of a Legendary Partnership](https://www.fool.com/investing/2023/11/28/charlie-munger-appreciation/); [Stripe Press – Poor Charlie's Almanack](https://www.stripe.press/poor-charlies-almanack/book).

### 1.2 1973–1974 — The 53% drawdown and decision to wind up Wheeler, Munger

- **Years:** 1973–1974 (drawdowns), 1975–76 (wind-up)
- **Context:** Limited partners' accounts dropped 31.9% in 1973 and 31.5% in 1974 — cumulatively, $1,000 invested on 1 Jan 1973 became $467 by 1 Jan 1975, a >53% drawdown. The partnership recovered strongly in 1975 (+73.2%), but he chose to wind it up in 1976 anyway.
- **Decision:** Wound up Wheeler, Munger & Co. after the recovery, returning capital to LPs.
- **Stated reasoning:** "We got drubbed by the 1973 to 1974 crash, not in terms of true underlying value, but by quoted market value, as our publicly traded securities had to be marked down to below half of what they were really worth." The deeper reason was psychological: when dealing with his own money, losses didn't bother him much, but reported losses to LPs caused him "tremendous pain."
- **Outcome:** Recovered the drawdown but voluntarily exited the LP business. Pivoted toward holding-company structure (Blue Chip / Wesco / Berkshire) where mark-to-market reporting to outsiders did not bind him.
- **Retrospective reflection:** Treated the experience as confirmation that he should never run other people's money under quarterly reporting again. The lesson he extracted was structural — *do not put yourself in a position where you are forced to explain quotational losses to others* — not "I should have invested differently."
- **Framework consistency: HIGH for outcome, REVEALING for cause.** He preached equanimity about price quotations ("Mr Market is your servant, not your master"), but in practice he could not stand reporting LP losses. The decision reveals that his framework was built for permanent capital (Berkshire, Daily Journal) precisely because his temperament could not bear the alternative.
- Sources: [GuruFocus – A Few Takeaways from Revisiting Munger's Partnership](https://www.gurufocus.com/news/262281/a-few-takeaways-from-revisiting-mungers-partnership); [Yahoo Finance – Lessons From Charlie Munger's Early Partnership](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/lessons-charlie-mungers-early-partnership-164823987.html).

### 1.3 1975–1978 — Joining Berkshire as Vice Chairman

- **Years:** 1975 (Blue Chip absorbed into Berkshire); formal Vice Chairman from 1978.
- **Context:** Munger and Buffett had spent the early 1970s building Blue Chip Stamps and using it to acquire Wesco Financial (1973), See's Candies (1972), and Buffalo Evening News (1977). Blue Chip had taken an 8% stake in Wesco; when Wesco's board considered merging with Financial Corporation of Santa Barbara at terms Munger and Buffett thought unfair, they intervened to kill the deal and ultimately took 80% of Wesco at $17. The SEC investigated price manipulation. Blue Chip paid ~$115K to former Wesco investors with no admission of guilt.
- **Decision:** After SEC pressure, fully consolidated Blue Chip into Berkshire in 1975; Munger became Vice President, then Vice Chairman in 1978. Took almost no salary — paid in equity/rolling-up stake. Continued to run Wesco as a separate listed vehicle until Berkshire absorbed it fully in 2011.
- **Stated reasoning:** Architectural simplicity, alignment of interests, preference for equity over salary. Wanted decision authority over a permanent-capital vehicle, not management of an LP.
- **Outcome:** Berkshire compounded into one of the largest companies in the world. Munger's stake made him a billionaire without ever taking material salary.
- **Retrospective reflection:** Repeatedly cited Berkshire's structure (permanent capital, no debt, decentralised) as the architectural decision that mattered most.
- **Framework consistency: HIGH.** Equity-only compensation, long horizon, no LP reporting — the structural opposite of Wheeler, Munger.
- Sources: [Charlie Munger – Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger); [Wesco Financial – Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wesco_Financial); [Chestnut Street – #29: The Man Who Whispered To Warren Buffett](https://chestnutstreet.substack.com/p/29-the-man-who-whispered-to-warren).

## 2. Investment decisions

### 2.1 1972 — See's Candies: the cigar-butt → quality-compounder shift

- **Year:** 1972
- **Context:** See's Candies — California boxed-chocolate maker — earning ~$4M pre-tax on $8M of net tangible assets. Asking price was $30M; Buffett offered $25M (a Graham-style cigar-butt would have demanded paying ≤book value).
- **Decision:** Munger persuaded Buffett to pay $25M for See's despite the price being well above book — the *first time* Buffett ratified buying a wonderful business at a fair price rather than a fair business at a wonderful price.
- **Stated reasoning (Munger):** Pricing power and brand were unrecorded balance-sheet assets. "Forget what you know about buying fair businesses at wonderful prices; instead, buy wonderful businesses at fair prices." The moat — Christmas-gift habit, geographic dominance, family-recipe brand — would compound for decades without requiring incremental capital.
- **Outcome:** See's has produced "well over $2B" in cumulative profits on the original $25M investment.
- **Retrospective reflection (Buffett, repeatedly):** "If we hadn't bought See's, we wouldn't have bought Coke." The See's purchase recoded Buffett's mental model of value, making the Coca-Cola, Apple, and quality-bank investments possible.
- **Framework consistency: HIGH.** This is *the* decision that defines the espoused framework. The framework didn't pre-exist — the decision created it.
- Sources: [Fortune – How Charlie Munger built the blueprint for Berkshire's $785B empire](https://fortune.com/2023/11/28/charlie-munger-investment-strategy-warren-buffetts-cigar-butt-berkshire-hathaway/); [Acquirer's Multiple – How Charlie Convinced Me To Break My Cigar-Butt Habit](https://acquirersmultiple.com/2017/02/warren-buffett-how-charlie-convinced-me-to-break-my-cigar-butt-investing-habit/); [SBO – See's Candies financial teardown](https://sbo.financial/blog/financial-teardowns/sees-candies-a-financial-teardown/).

### 2.2 2008 — BYD (Chinese EV / battery maker)

- **Year:** September 2008
- **Context:** Berkshire's stated rule: don't buy what you don't understand; tech is out of the circle. BYD was a Chinese battery-and-car company most US investors had never heard of. Li Lu (Munger's protégé) brought it.
- **Decision:** Berkshire bought 225M shares (~8.25% stake) for ~$270M. Munger championed it personally; Buffett deferred to Munger's judgement.
- **Stated reasoning:** Bet on the founder, Wang Chuanfu — "a combination of Thomas Edison and Jack Welch." Munger: "I don't want to bet against 17,000 Chinese engineers led by Wang Chuanfu, plus 100,000 more talented Chinese in a brand-new area."
- **Outcome:** Initial $270M grew to ~$8–9B at peak. Munger called it "the best investment we've ever helped make at Berkshire."
- **Retrospective reflection:** Doubled down on the founder thesis. Compared Wang favourably to Elon Musk, calling BYD's lead over Tesla "almost ridiculous."
- **Framework consistency: LOW (rule-breaking) → HIGH (in retrospect).** Broke "no tech," "no China political risk," "no early-stage capital-intensive industries." Justified by founder quality + circle-of-competence-via-Li-Lu. Reveals: *the espoused rules are heuristics, not laws — when one human's track record dominates the entire risk profile, all other rules bend.*
- Sources: [Yahoo Finance – Munger says BYD was his best investment](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/charlie-munger-says-byd-best-094339069.html); [Rational Walk – Berkshire's Investment in BYD: A Bet on Wang Chuan-Fu](https://rationalwalk.com/berkshires-investment-in-byd-a-bet-on-wang-chuan-fu/); [Fortune – Munger says BYD success vs. Tesla is 'almost ridiculous'](https://fortune.com/2023/02/16/charlie-munger-byd-stake-warren-buffett-berkshire-hathaway-china-tesla/).

### 2.3 1997–2023 — Costco: never sold a share

- **Years:** Joined board 1997; held until death 2023.
- **Context:** Came across via Sol Price (Price Club founder, later merged with Costco). Costco asked Buffett to join the board; Buffett declined and sent Munger.
- **Decision:** Joined board in 1997 and personally never sold a share over 26 years. Berkshire exited its institutional Costco position in late 2020; Munger publicly disagreed and kept his personal holding intact.
- **Stated reasoning:** "I love everything about Costco. I'm a total addict, and I'm never going to sell a share." The model: lowest unit price, biggest efficient stores, membership filter, reward points. A culture-and-system moat, not a brand moat.
- **Outcome:** Costco compounded at ~14% annually for 25+ years post-Munger joining the board; one of the great post-WWII US compounders.
- **Retrospective reflection:** In his final Acquired interview (Oct 2023), Munger called Berkshire's 2020 exit from Costco one of the firm's biggest errors — "the big Costco error."
- **Framework consistency: HIGH.** Quintessential expression of "find a great business and sit on your ass." The disagreement with Berkshire on the 2020 exit shows Munger's individual conviction outranked even institutional alignment.
- Sources: [Yahoo Finance – Berkshire Hathaway's Charlie Munger had a longtime, special relationship with Costco](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/berkshire-hathaways-charlie-munger-had-a-longtime-special-relationship-with-costco-223417032.html); [Morningstar – Final Thoughts From Charlie Munger on Apple, Warren Buffett, and the Big Costco Error](https://www.morningstar.com/stocks/final-thoughts-charlie-munger-apple-warren-buffett-big-costco-error); [Costco IR – Passing of Long-Time Board Member, Charles Munger](https://investor.costco.com/news/news-details/2023/Costco-Wholesale-Corporation-Reports-November-Sales-Results-and-the-Passing-of-Long-Time-Board-Member-Charles-Munger/default.aspx).

### 2.4 2016–2022 — Wells Fargo: held while Berkshire dumped

- **Years:** 2016 fake-accounts scandal; Berkshire began selling pre-pandemic and exited in 2022; Munger personally never sold post-2017.
- **Context:** Wells Fargo had been Berkshire's preferred bank for ~30 years. In Sept 2016, the cross-selling fraud broke — millions of fake accounts opened by branch staff under sales-quota pressure.
- **Decision (Munger):** Characterised the scandal as a "glitch." "If I had to say which bank is more likely to behave the best in the future, it might be Wells Fargo out of all of them." Held his Daily Journal Wells Fargo position. Diverged from Buffett, who gradually sold Berkshire's stake to zero by 2022.
- **Stated reasoning:** "The truth of the matter is they made a business judgment that was wrong. They got so caught up in cross-selling and incentive systems that some people reacted badly and did things they shouldn't." Munger said he was "a little more lenient" on bankers than Buffett and "expected less out of bankers."
- **Outcome:** Wells Fargo underperformed materially over 2016–2023. Munger's loyalty cost the Daily Journal portfolio meaningfully relative to a clean exit.
- **Retrospective reflection:** Never publicly recanted on Wells Fargo — even when the position lagged. His framing remained: incentive system bug, not character defect, and management was being fixed.
- **Framework consistency: MEDIUM-LOW.** Espoused "lollapalooza" framework would predict that perverse incentive systems produce systemic, not isolated, failures — exactly the Wells Fargo case. The decision to hold contradicts his own model on incentive-driven misbehaviour. Reveals: *Munger had stronger loyalty bias to long-held positions than his framework prescribes.*
- Sources: [CNBC – Charlie Munger thinks Wells Fargo's huge fake accounts scandal was a 'glitch'](https://www.cnbc.com/2017/02/15/charlie-munger-just-gave-wells-fargo-a-big-vote-of-confidence.html); [American Banker – Charlie Munger's Berkshire legacy mixed banking critique with investments](https://www.americanbanker.com/news/charlie-mungers-berkshire-legacy-mixed-banking-critique-with-investments); [Yahoo Finance – Charlie Munger on Why He Still Holds Wells Fargo](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/charlie-munger-why-still-holds-164425166.html).

### 2.5 2006–2014 — Tesco (UK): "huge mistake"

- **Years:** Bought 2006; sold down 2014 after the £263M accounting overstatement scandal.
- **Context:** Berkshire built a meaningful Tesco position during UK retail expansion; held through multiple deteriorating quarters.
- **Decision:** Held too long. Tesco fell 49% in a year on revelations the company had overstated H1 profit by £263M.
- **Stated reasoning at entry:** Quality UK grocery franchise, durable cash flows, reasonable price.
- **Stated reasoning at exit:** Buffett: "An attentive investor, I'm embarrassed to report, would have sold Tesco shares earlier. I made a big mistake with this investment by dawdling." Buffett took the public hit; Munger commented less publicly but the position was a Berkshire decision Munger had ratified.
- **Outcome:** Reported pre-tax loss of $678M on the position.
- **Retrospective reflection:** Buffett: "That was a huge mistake by me." Specifically named "dawdling" — slow exit on deteriorating fundamentals — as the failure mode.
- **Framework consistency: LOW.** Espoused framework: when the thesis breaks, exit fast. Reality: held through multiple disappointments. This is the *anti-Munger* failure mode of inertia on losing positions — a known psychology bias (status quo + commitment) that he taught about but didn't fully escape.
- Sources: [Motley Fool – Warren Buffett Says That Mistakes Are OK](https://www.fool.com/investing/general/2014/10/19/warren-buffett-says-that-mistakes-are-ok.aspx); [Nasdaq – Warren Buffett's Failures: 15 Investing Mistakes He Regrets](https://www.nasdaq.com/articles/warren-buffetts-failures-15-investing-mistakes-he-regrets-2017-12-06); [CNBC – Warren Buffett's failures: 15 investing mistakes](https://www.cnbc.com/2017/12/15/warren-buffetts-failures-15-investing-mistakes-he-regrets-2017-12-06.html).

### 2.6 2021–2023 — Alibaba (Daily Journal)

- **Years:** Q1 2021 entry; Q4 2021 peak; Q1 2022 cut by half; later trimmed further; Munger died with the position still in DJCO portfolio.
- **Context:** Munger built the Alibaba position personally through Daily Journal during the China internet drawdown post-Ant Group IPO suspension (Nov 2020) and Jack Ma's disappearance from public view. Initial 165,320 shares Q1 2021; peak 602,060 shares Q4 2021. Bought partly on margin — unusual for him.
- **Decision sequence:** Aggressively built into the regulatory crackdown -> admitted error -> cut by half in Q1 2022 -> continued holding the rest.
- **Stated reasoning at entry:** "Their position in the Chinese internet" — i.e., dominant ecosystem player at low valuation.
- **Stated reasoning at trim:** Three explicit admissions:
  1. *Misclassification:* "I got charmed by the idea of their position in the Chinese internet; I didn't stop to realize they're still a goddamn retailer." (Daily Journal AGM 2023)
  2. *Geopolitical/political risk:* Jack Ma's comments offended the Chinese government; the political risk cascade was real and persistent.
  3. *Leverage:* The position had been bought with margin — violating his own no-leverage rule.
- **Outcome:** Sold the trimmed half at a loss. Position remained in DJCO portfolio at his death; recovered post-mortem.
- **Retrospective reflection:** Among the cleanest mea culpas of his career. Named all three errors explicitly, didn't blame the market.
- **Framework consistency: LOW at entry, HIGH at retrospection.** Three espoused rules broken simultaneously: (1) circle-of-competence (Chinese internet is not US/HK retail), (2) no leverage, (3) understand the actual business model not just the moat narrative. Reveals: *Even in his late 90s, Munger could be charmed by a thesis at a price — and the broken framework was visible to him within 12 months.*
- Sources: [Fortune – Warren Buffett pal Charlie Munger was an Alibaba bull—What changed his mind?](https://fortune.com/2022/04/14/warren-buffett-charlie-munger-alibaba-shares-investment-daily-journal-sale/); [Yahoo Finance – Charlie Munger Called Alibaba 'One of the Worst Mistakes I Ever Made'](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/charlie-munger-called-alibaba-one-230116375.html); [GuruFocus – Munger's Alibaba Regrets](https://www.gurufocus.com/news/1958954/mungers-alibaba-regrets).

### 2.7 2011–2017 — IBM (Berkshire)

- **Years:** Buffett initiated 2011; fully exited 2017–18.
- **Context:** Buffett built a ~$10B IBM stake from 2011 — a tech investment justified as "no longer really a tech company" but a services/consulting firm with predictable cash flows. Munger reportedly skeptical from the start.
- **Decision:** Buffett bought; Munger's role appears to have been watchful neutrality. Berkshire exited fully by 2018 at a loss/break-even.
- **Stated reasoning (Buffett):** Predictable cash flows, durable customer base, share buybacks compounding value-per-share for patient holders.
- **Outcome:** IBM failed to adapt to cloud transition; revenue stagnated; Berkshire exited and rotated into Apple. Buffett publicly admitted being wrong on IBM in 2017.
- **Retrospective reflection (Munger):** Apple was the right tech investment because it's "more of a consumer goods company." IBM was misclassified as predictable when it was actually being disrupted.
- **Framework consistency for Munger: HIGH.** His skepticism aligned with the espoused circle-of-competence rule for tech. Reveals that Munger was willing to defer to Buffett on conviction calls even when uncomfortable — and right.
- Sources: [Yahoo Finance – Warren Buffett and IBM: A Very Profitable Learning Experience](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/warren-buffett-ibm-very-profitable-171245620.html); [CNBC – This decade saw Warren Buffett finally turn his back on IBM](https://www.cnbc.com/2019/12/31/this-decade-saw-warren-buffett-finally-exit-ibm-jump-big-into-apple.html); [Fox Business – Berkshire Meeting: Munger on Buffett's Apple Investment](https://www.foxbusiness.com/features/berkshire-meeting-munger-on-buffetts-apple-investment-either-youve-gone-crazy-or-youre-learning).

## 3. Anti-decisions (what he chose NOT to do)

### 3.1 Refused to invest in tech (with BYD/Apple as exceptions)

- **Decision:** Systematic avoidance for 50+ years.
- **Stated reasoning:** Outside circle of competence; cannot predict 5–10 year economics of tech businesses; competitive dynamics shift too fast; "too hard pile."
- **Exceptions:** BYD (founder bet via Li Lu); Apple (reframed as consumer goods); not IBM (which he was skeptical of).
- **Framework consistency: HIGH.** Exceptions were principled, not opportunistic — each violated the rule for a specific articulable reason. The pattern is "rules are heuristics; exceptions need named, defensible reasons."
- Sources: [25iq – Why does Charlie Munger not invest in high-technology businesses?](https://25iq.com/2015/12/19/why-does-charlie-munger-say-he-avoids-investing-in-high-technology-businesses/).

### 3.2 Refused to short stocks systematically

- **Decision:** Never ran a short book despite identifying frauds (e.g., publicly ridiculed Valeant, Wirecard-style structures, crypto exchanges).
- **Stated reasoning:** "It's much more fun to watch somebody you like and admire succeeding than watching some jerk half-mismanage some company that's very cheap... It's the reason we don't short stocks. Even if we made a lot of money doing it, I don't think either one of us would bother." And: "You're crazy if you're rich and deliberately go out and do a lot of unpleasant things you don't have to."
- **Outcome:** Avoided large losses from short-squeeze episodes; also forfeited large potential gains from high-profile fraud unwinds.
- **Framework consistency: HIGH for psychology, MEDIUM for capital allocation.** The reasoning is explicitly hedonic ("unpleasant"), not financial. Reveals: *Munger's framework was always integrated with his preferred lifestyle — capital decisions had to also be lived-with-decisions. He treated his attention as a non-renewable resource.*
- Sources: [Benzinga – Charlie Munger Once Explained Why He And Warren Buffett Refuse To Short Stocks](https://www.benzinga.com/markets/tech/25/11/48578367/charlie-munger-once-explained-why-he-and-warren-buffett-refuse-to-short-stocks-and-it-started-with-latters-tendency-to-invest-in-lousy-companies); [CMQ Investing – Buffett & Munger on Short Selling](https://cmqinvesting.substack.com/p/-warren-buffett-and-charlie-munger).

### 3.3 Refused to forecast macro / interest rates

- **Decision:** Made no asset-allocation calls based on macro forecasts.
- **Stated reasoning:** "Gigantic macroeconomic predictions are something I've never made any money on, and neither has Warren." And: "We don't believe anyone can accurately predict where interest rates are going, where GDP growth is going to be, what the Federal Reserve will do." When asked which macro stats he watches: "None. I find that what I see on the companies we monitor pretty much informs me of what is happening at the macro level." On forecasts in general: "Projections are put together by people who have an interest in a particular outcome, have a subconscious bias, and its apparent precision makes it fallacious."
- **Framework consistency: HIGH.** Bottom-up by construction. The reasoning has a Bayesian flavour: too many variables, relationships unstable, observers biased.
- Sources: [Sure Dividend – The 60 Best Charlie Munger Quotes](https://www.suredividend.com/charlie-munger-quotes/); [CNBC – Charlie Munger: Be afraid when a democracy thinks it can print money](https://www.cnbc.com/2019/05/06/charlie-munger-be-afraid-when-a-democracy-thinks-it-can-print-money-to-solve-all-its-problems.html).

### 3.4 Refused to diversify Daily Journal portfolio (concentrated in 4 names)

- **Decision:** Daily Journal portfolio at his death held only Wells Fargo, Bank of America, Alibaba, US Bancorp.
- **Stated reasoning:** "The idea of diversification makes sense to a point if you don't know what you're doing." If good ideas are rare, diversifying dilutes them. Risk comes from not knowing what you own, not from owning a lot of it.
- **Framework consistency: HIGH.** Maximally consistent with espoused doctrine. The Alibaba mis-classification (§2.6) shows the cost: when one of four positions is wrong, the dilution of the other three matters.
- Sources: [Novel Investor – Charlie Munger on Concentration](https://novelinvestor.com/charlie-munger-on-concentration/); [The Motley Fool – Charlie Munger's Investment Strategy](https://www.fool.com/investing/how-to-invest/famous-investors/charlie-munger/); [Daily Journal Corp portfolio holdings](https://blog.valuesense.io/daily-journal-corporation-portfolio-holdings/).

## 4. Personal decisions

### 4.1 1953 — Divorce from Nancy Huggins

- **Year:** 1953 (he was 29)
- **Context:** Married young in 1945; relationship deteriorated through the late 40s. Two-Nancy biographical confusion exists in some sources — first wife was Nancy Huggins, second was Nancy Barry (Borthwick).
- **Decision:** Divorce. Nancy retained the house and most assets.
- **Outcome:** Munger moved into "dreadful bachelor digs"; drove a poorly-repainted yellow Pontiac. Two years later, son Teddy was diagnosed with leukemia. Divorce stigma was severe in 1953.
- **Retrospective reflection:** Treated as a low point but never publicly elaborated; Munger preferred not to discuss the marriage. Married Nancy Borthwick in 1956; they remained married until her death in 2010.
- **Framework consistency: N/A — pre-framework.** Predates his investing career and reflective philosophy. Shaped subsequent insistence on stability and family compounding.
- Sources: [Charlie Munger – Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger); [Strictly Weddings – Charlie Munger Marriages and Divorces](https://www.strictlyweddings.com/info/notable-marriages/charlie-munger-182522); [News24 – Bill Gates chose 97-year-old Charlie Munger as his divorce lawyer](https://www.news24.com/you/news/international/this-is-why-bill-gates-chose-97-year-old-charlie-munger-as-his-divorce-lawyer-20210507).

### 4.2 1955 — Son Teddy died of leukemia at age 9

- **Year:** 1955 (Munger was 31)
- **Context:** Leukemia in 1955 was effectively 100% fatal. One year between diagnosis and death. Concurrent with divorce aftermath.
- **Decision (in his framing):** Choose not to be destroyed. Choose to function. Choose to not let one tragedy compound into multiple.
- **Stated reasoning:** "I cried all the time when my first child died. But I knew I couldn't change the fate." And later: "You should never, when facing some unbelievable tragedy, let one tragedy increase to two or three through your failure of will." And: "What mankind and civilization did was soldier through those tough years that took away my cousin Tommy from meningitis, and then took away my son Teddy from leukemia."
- **Outcome:** Source of his lifelong stoicism. Found meaning in subsequent medical advances — the leukemia cure rate has transformed since 1955.
- **Retrospective reflection:** This is the origin event for the "iron prescription": "If you just take the attitude that however bad it is in any way, it's always your fault and you just fix it as best you can — the so-called 'iron prescription' — I think that really works." And: "Self-pity is not going to help."
- **Framework consistency: FOUNDATIONAL.** This is where the framework comes from. The stoicism wasn't read in books and then applied; it was forged in 1955 and rationalised later.
- Sources: [Joshua Kennon – If Charlie Munger Didn't Quit When He Was Divorced, Broke, and Burying His 9 Year Old Son](https://www.joshuakennon.com/if-charlie-munger-didnt-quit-when-he-was-divorced-broke-and-burying-his-9-year-old-son/); [Safal Niveshak – A Story of Courage and Hope from the Life of Charlie Munger](https://www.safalniveshak.com/courage-and-hope-life-of-charlie-munger/); [Yahoo Finance – Charlie Munger Lost His 9-Year-Old Son To Cancer, Went Blind In One Eye](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/charlie-munger-lost-9-old-155612903.html).

### 4.3 1980 — Lost left eye to botched cataract surgery

- **Year:** 1980 (he was 56)
- **Context:** Routine cataract surgery on the left eye produced a complication ("epithelial downgrowth" / similar). The eye filled with blood and caused agonising pain.
- **Decision:** Asked the surgeon to remove the entire eye rather than continue treatment. When doctors warned the right eye might develop the same complication and leave him fully blind, **he did not refuse the surgery on the right eye out of fear, and he started taking braille lessons** to prepare for total blindness. He chose preparation over hope.
- **Stated reasoning:** "Some people just naturally complain and other people just naturally put their head down and soldier through it. Warren and I believe in just soldiering through it without too much fuss. I have the theory that the dumbest thing you can ever do in life is to feel like a victim."
- **Outcome:** Right eye remained intact; never went fully blind; functioned with one eye for 43 more years.
- **Retrospective reflection:** Cited as a real-life test of the iron prescription. The braille lessons (taking action on the worst-case while continuing daily life) became a personal example he returned to in speeches.
- **Framework consistency: HIGH and load-bearing.** The behaviour was not "stay positive" but "prepare for the worst case while not pre-grieving." Probabilistic stoicism, not optimism.
- Sources: [MyUnderstanding – Inspiring Story: Charlie Munger at his 50's](https://myunderstanding.substack.com/p/inspiring-story-charlie-munger-at); [Kingswell – Soldiering Through Life](https://www.kingswell.io/p/soldier-through); [25iq – How does Charlie Munger recommend dealing with adversity?](https://25iq.com/2015/11/14/charlie-munger-ama-how-does-charlie-munger-recommend-dealing-with-adversity/).

### 4.4 Architectural donations — Stanford GSB, Michigan Law, UCSB Munger Hall

- **Years:** Multiple donations across 1990s–2020s; UCSB Munger Hall most controversial (2021).
- **Context:** Munger donated heavily to universities on the explicit condition that the buildings follow his designs exactly. UCSB Munger Hall: $200M donation; planned 11-story, 1.68M-sq-ft, 4,500-bed dorm with most rooms windowless. Inspired (by his account) by the windowless cabin design on Disney cruise ships — to push residents into common spaces.
- **Decision:** Insisted on personal architectural vision over expert input. Distinguished consultant Dennis McFadden (FAIA) resigned from UCSB's design review committee in protest, writing that the design "ignores documented evidence" on natural light and wellbeing. UCSB cancelled Munger Hall in 2023 after his death.
- **Stated reasoning:** Believed the trade-off (smaller windowless rooms in exchange for more / better common space) would produce better student outcomes than conventional dorms. Wanted to force interaction by making rooms unattractive.
- **Outcome:** Stanford GSB and Michigan Law buildings were built. UCSB Munger Hall was cancelled. Public reception ranged from dismissive to harshly critical.
- **Retrospective reflection:** Munger never publicly recanted; defended the design as an efficiency-and-social-design choice rooted in observation (Disney cabins -> people are fine without natural light when the alternative is a great common space).
- **Framework consistency: REVEALING.** The first-principles, "I observed how this works elsewhere therefore the conventional wisdom is wrong" reasoning is pure Munger. But the espoused framework also includes "respect specialised expertise where you lack it" — and architects are specialists in human-environment interaction. This is a case where his confidence in cross-domain reasoning *outran* his deference to domain expertise. Reveals: *the strength that produced the See's insight (cross-domain reasoning beats narrow expertise) became a weakness when the domain experts were actually right.*

## 5. Late-life decisions

### 5.1 2022–2023 — Doubled down on crypto criticism

- **Years:** Multiple statements 2022–early 2023.
- **Context:** Crypto bull-then-collapse cycle: 2021 peak, 2022 crash, FTX collapse Nov 2022. Munger was in his late 90s, with no career/reputation incentive to soften.
- **Decision:** Repeatedly and publicly attacked crypto with escalating language rather than going quiet.
- **Stated reasoning:**
  - "Crypto is a bad combination of fraud and delusion... a lot of delusion. Partly fraud and partly delusion. That's a bad combination."
  - "In my life, I try to avoid things that are stupid and evil and make me look bad — and bitcoin does all three."
    - *Stupid* "because it's still likely to go to zero"
    - *Evil* "because it undermines the Federal Reserve System"
    - *Makes us look foolish* "compared to the Communist leader in China" (referring to China's crypto ban)
  - "The country did not need a currency that was good for kidnappers."
  - "People who oppose my position are idiots." (Feb 2023, CNBC)
  - "Good ideas, carried to wretched excess, become bad ideas."
- **Outcome:** Bitcoin recovered post-2022 to new highs in 2024. Munger died with the position empirically wrong on price but possibly correct on character.
- **Retrospective reflection:** Never softened. Chose the clarity-of-position trade-off over hedging.
- **Framework consistency: HIGH on principle, RISKY on epistemics.** "Stupid + evil + looks bad" is a fast clear filter — vintage Munger. But "people who oppose me are idiots" violates his own dictum that you should be able to articulate the other side's position better than they can. Reveals: *late-life Munger optimised for clarity-of-stance over epistemic humility, possibly because he no longer needed to update.*

### 5.2 2023 — Worked until ~3 weeks before death (age 99)

- **Year:** 2023
- **Context:** Daily Journal AGM Feb 2023 (3-hour Q&A at age 99). Acquired podcast — his only longform podcast interview ever — recorded and released October 30, 2023. Died November 28, 2023.
- **Decision:** Continued to work, give interviews, sit on the Costco board, and chair the Daily Journal. Did not retire, did not slow down.
- **Stated reasoning:** Implicit — work was the meaningful activity. He repeatedly joked about wishing he'd compounded longer. The Acquired interview was given roughly 4 weeks before his death; he was sharp and detailed throughout.

## Cross-cutting observations on framework vs reality

Five places where the espoused doctrine and the actual decisions diverged:

1. **Wheeler, Munger 1973–74 wind-up.** Espoused: equanimity about price quotations. Actual: closed the partnership because he could not bear LP reporting losses. Framework was reverse-engineered from temperament, not the other way around.

2. **Wells Fargo post-2016.** Espoused: incentive systems produce systemic failure (lollapalooza). Actual: held through the lollapalooza of his own model and called it a "glitch." Loyalty to long-held positions overrode the framework.

3. **Tesco 2006–14.** Espoused: exit fast on broken thesis. Actual: dawdled. Inertia and commitment beat the rule.

4. **Alibaba 2021.** Espoused: circle-of-competence, no leverage, understand the actual business. Actual: violated all three at once. The framework was correctly applied *after* the fact, not during.

5. **UCSB Munger Hall.** Espoused: respect specialised expertise. Actual: overrode it on architecture. Cross-domain confidence — the strength that produced See's — was the same trait that produced the windowless dorm.

The decisions show the framework as a *retrospective rationalisation device* as much as an action-guiding system. Munger's real decision algorithm appears to have been: (a) deep trust in a small set of people (Buffett, Wang Chuanfu, Sol Price/Costco management, Li Lu); (b) extreme stoicism on irrecoverable losses (Teddy, eye, divorce); (c) clarity-of-stance over hedging in public; (d) loyalty bias to long-held positions that occasionally cost him; (e) willingness to break stated rules when one variable (founder, moat, structure) dominated all others.

The most *revealing* fact is item (a): in every framework-breaking investment that worked (BYD, See's, Costco), there was a person whose judgement Munger trusted absolutely. In every framework-breaking decision that didn't work (Alibaba, Wells Fargo hold, UCSB), the trust was either misplaced or his own.
