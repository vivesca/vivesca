# Cursus — Career Principles

From *cursus honorum* — the Roman framework for career advancement. Holds durable principles for career communication and professional positioning.

**Not user-invocable.** Consult when doing career-adjacent work: outreach, onboarding prep, networking, LinkedIn, job search, first impressions, or any interaction where Terry is asking for something from a career contact.

The `message` skill owns the mechanics (how to draft and send). This skill owns the principles (what makes the draft worth sending).

---

## 1. No Blank Asks

Before drafting any ask to a career contact — pre-reading, advice, intro, referral, feedback — run `cerno "<topic>"` and a quick web search first. Then frame as a specific gap:

> "I've reviewed X and Y — is there Z I'm missing?"

Never: "Any advice?" / "Anything I should read?" / "Thoughts?"

A blank ask signals you haven't done the work. A specific ask signals you have. The research *is* the ask.

## 2. Show, Don't Tell

Specific evidence over claims in every message, intro, and profile:

- **Not:** "I'm strong at AI governance"
- **Yes:** "I built CNCBI's first GenAI solution and was selected for HKMA's inaugural GenAI Sandbox — one of 10 banks"

Claims are forgettable. Evidence is specific and verifiable. The more concrete, the more credible.

## 3. Narrative Consistency

One leaving story, told identically to every party (Capco, CNCBI, references, network):

> "New leadership had a different strategic direction for the function. We agreed it was time for a change. I'm grateful for what I built there."

Never deviate. Inconsistency travels. Applies to any positioning narrative — career arc, specialisation, why Capco, why AI governance. Pick the frame, repeat it exactly.

## 4. Network Capital

Networks have a balance you spend and rebuild:

- **Warm > cold** — always. A warm intro is worth 10 cold messages.
- **Don't spend capital on a weak ask** — if the ask isn't specific and researched (see #1), you've spent capital for nothing.
- **Touch before asks** — comment, share, congratulate before asking. Don't activate dormant contacts only when you need them.
- **Senior contacts are finite** — use them for high-leverage moments: key intros, reference calls, strategic advice. Don't burn Bertie on questions you can answer with web search.

## 5. Pre-Engagement Prep

Before any career conversation — coffee chat, Day 1, client intro, recruiter call, 1:1 — run:

1. `cerno "<person or topic>"` — what does the vault already know?
2. LinkedIn — recent posts, role changes, company signals
3. One specific observation or question drawn from that research

Walking in with a specific observation sets a different frame than walking in empty.

## 6. First Impression Window

The first 30 days at a new role set the frame for the next 3 years:

- **Over-deliver on the first visible deliverable** — one strong early piece is worth months of average work later
- **Listen more than you speak** — map the landscape before staking positions
- **Find the real problem** — the stated brief is rarely the full brief; ask "what does success look like in 6 months?" in week one
- **Be seen thinking, not just doing** — share observations and hypotheses, not only completed tasks

## 7. Specific Beats General

Every dimension of career communication improves with specificity:

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "Any advice?" | "I've read X — is there Y I'm missing?" |
| "I have ML experience" | "I shipped CNCBI's first ML model in production" |
| "What does AI governance look like at Capco?" | "How do you structure an AI risk tiering assessment for a tier-1 bank?" |
| "What do you think?" | "Does this framing hold given what HKMA signalled in the Oct 2025 sandbox report?" |
