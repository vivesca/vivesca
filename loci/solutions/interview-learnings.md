# Interview Learnings

## Recruiter framing vs hiring manager reality

Giselle (recruiter) pitched DBS Data Governance role as "technical GenAI person to challenge the team." Alison (hiring manager, SVP) revealed it's actually her deputy doing governance process — ~20-30% AI, ~70-80% traditional data governance. Other teams handle GenAI solution design.

**Pattern:** Recruiter pitch optimises for candidate attraction, not accuracy. Always probe the hiring manager directly on day-to-day scope. Key question: "How much of this role is X vs Y day-to-day?"

**Source:** DBS R1 debrief, Feb 16 2026.

## VP-level weakness framing

Naming core VP competencies (communication, project management) as weaknesses undermines candidacy at that level. Better: name weaknesses that are real but reinforce your transition narrative.

For builder-to-governance transition:
- "I default to building the fix rather than designing the control" — honest, self-aware, and the weakness IS the strength for the role
- "I can be impatient with process when I see a faster path" — same energy

**Pattern:** Weaknesses should be real, show self-awareness, and ideally become strengths in the specific role you're interviewing for.

**Source:** DBS R1 debrief, Feb 16 2026.

## Gap analysis reframe for auditors

If asked "have you done gap analysis?" — don't concede. Gap analysis IS what auditors do: compare actual state against a standard, identify gaps, recommend remediation. PwC + DBS IA = years of it. Just not on AI regulatory circulars specifically. The methodology is transferable.

**Pattern:** Don't undersell transferable skills by anchoring to the specific domain rather than the methodology.

**Source:** DBS R1 debrief, Feb 16 2026.
