# LinkedIn Comment Craft — Lessons

## Comment on the POST, not the topic

The #1 mistake across 3 iterations: writing a comment that engages the author's broader work (blog posts, ideas) rather than the specific thing they posted. People see the post, then the comment. If they don't connect, you look like you're using someone's thread to talk about your own thing.

**Test:** Would this comment make sense to someone who ONLY read the post and nothing else?

## Engage the product mechanic, not just the concept

v1 and v2 both talked about "outbound agents" as a concept. The post was about a specific product with a specific setup (procedure + CSV). Engaging the mechanic ("how are you handling data recency on the CSV side?") shows you read the product, not just the headline.

## Questions > statements for founder's launch posts

"I'd be curious how you're thinking about X" invites a reply.
"X introduces compliance risks" shuts down conversation and lectures a CTO about their own product.
On LinkedIn: questions that demonstrate expertise > statements that demonstrate expertise.

## Don't explain someone's domain back to them

"This is a harder problem than most people realise" tells a CTO who built the thing that his problem is hard. He knows. Flip to validation: "The outbound framing is the key insight here" — same content, different posture (agreeing with authority vs teaching from above).

## Consilium mode for reputation-building content

Public comments, LinkedIn posts, anything visible to future clients/employers → full council with --domain and --persona. Not --quick. Updated in consilium skill (Feb 2026).

## Include source material in draft reviews

When reviewing drafts via consilium, always include: (1) the original post/thread, (2) the draft, (3) relationship context, (4) review criteria. Updated in consilium skill (Feb 2026).

## Ship before the post fades

LinkedIn post engagement drops sharply after ~1 week. Three councils on a 2-week-old post was at the limit. One council with the right framing would have been enough. The polishing loop is real — recognise when you've crossed from "improving" to "delaying."

## Find the right angle before drafting — not after

Three full consilium passes iterating on bad drafts is waste. The right order: (1) brainstorm angles with consilium first, (2) pick the angle, (3) draft once, (4) one consilium review. Don't iterate on a wrong angle — find the right one upstream.

## Every AI angle needs scrutiny before posting

"AI people say X about quantum" sounds plausible but breaks fast under expert review. ML pipeline overhead from PQC: real but overstated. Training data encryption threat: partially wrong (AES-256 is quantum-resistant; threat is asymmetric/transport layer). If an AI angle can't survive a 30-second stress test ("is this actually true?"), drop it.

## Not every comment needs to showcase your primary identity

Terry is an AI guy. But if the post is about security/regulation and the authentic AI angle doesn't hold up, commenting on the regulatory signal is still valuable. "You don't need to say AI to be relevant as an AI leader" (GLM, consilium). Forcing it damages credibility more than omitting it.

## "Now live" ≠ "just announced" — verify operational status

HKMA QPI was *announced* Feb 3, 2026 with rollout "in coming months." Saying "now live" implies operational when it isn't quite. Always check: announced vs. live vs. effective date. One word change ("just announced") is worth catching before a CIO reads it.

## Regulatory acronym stacking kills LinkedIn comments

Council's gravitational pull is "add more regulations" (BCBS 239, NSFR, MCOB 7.6.4, SR 11-7). The judge killed all of it: "These are consultants consulting, not humans commenting." Rule of thumb: if you'd need to name a regulation to make the point land, save it for your own content.
