# running vs resistance training cognitive performance and sleep quality evidence review 2024 2025

# Running Versus Resistance Training for Cognitive Performance and Sleep Quality: A Comprehensive Evidence Review of Recent Research

This comprehensive report synthesizes recent evidence from 2024-2025 comparing the effects of running (aerobic exercise) and resistance training on cognitive performance and sleep quality. The analysis reveals that both exercise modalities provide significant cognitive and sleep benefits, but through distinct neurobiological mechanisms and with domain-specific advantages. **Aerobic exercise, particularly running, demonstrates superior effects on memory function and hippocampal health, while resistance training shows greater benefits for global cognition, inhibitory control, and attention**[1][13][13]. For sleep quality, resistance training shows a notable advantage with participants gaining approximately 40 minutes of sleep per night, compared to 23 minutes with aerobic exercise alone[9]. However, the optimal approach appears to be context-dependent, varying by individual factors including age, cognitive status, baseline fitness, and personal preferences. Recent network meta-analyses and randomized controlled trials from 2024-2025 provide unprecedented clarity on optimal exercise prescriptions, suggesting that moderate-intensity aerobic exercise performed 3-7 times weekly for 20-45 minutes yields optimal executive function benefits[1][4], while progressive resistance training twice weekly at 40-80% of one repetition maximum appears particularly beneficial for memory and attentional processes[1][15]. This report provides evidence-based guidance for practitioners and individuals seeking to optimize cognitive health and sleep quality through exercise interventions.

## Mechanisms Linking Exercise Modalities to Brain Health

### Neurobiological Pathways of Aerobic Exercise

The neurobiological effects of running and aerobic exercise operate through multiple interconnected pathways that collectively enhance brain health and cognitive function[1][20]. When individuals engage in aerobic activities such as running, cycling, or brisk walking, immediate physiological changes occur that initiate a cascade of beneficial neural adaptations. Aerobic exercise increases cerebral blood flow substantially, delivering greater quantities of oxygen and essential nutrients to brain regions critical for memory consolidation, attention, and executive control[1][42][45]. This enhanced perfusion is particularly pronounced in the hippocampus, a brain region fundamental to memory formation and learning, and the prefrontal cortex, which governs higher-order cognitive functions[1].

Beyond acute hemodynamic effects, aerobic exercise stimulates the production and release of crucial neurotrophic factors, most notably brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF)[1][20][42]. BDNF serves as a vital molecular mediator of neuroplasticity, the brain's capacity to reorganize and form new neural connections throughout life[20]. Studies have demonstrated that regular aerobic exercise increases BDNF expression in the hippocampus and other memory-critical regions, with effects persisting for hours following exercise cessation[1][20]. This elevation of BDNF promotes neurogenesis—the birth of new neurons—particularly in the dentate gyrus of the hippocampus, a process that directly translates to improved memory consolidation and retention[1][45]. Research indicates that aerobic exercise can increase hippocampal volume by approximately 2% over several months, reversing age-related volumetric loss that typically occurs at a rate of roughly 1% per year in sedentary older adults[45].

Aerobic exercise additionally exerts profound effects on neurotransmitter systems that regulate mood, cognition, and arousal[1]. The exercise-induced elevation of norepinephrine, a neurotransmitter released by the locus coeruleus—a brainstem region crucial for attention regulation—enhances alertness and focused attention[1]. Simultaneously, aerobic exercise increases dopamine availability in reward-processing circuits and augments serotonin levels, thereby improving mood and reducing depressive and anxious symptoms[42]. These neurotransmitter changes represent fundamental mechanisms through which aerobic exercise improves both cognitive performance and emotional regulation[1][42].

The anti-inflammatory effects of regular aerobic exercise represent another critical mechanism linking physical activity to improved brain health[1][7]. Chronic low-grade inflammation is increasingly recognized as a pathogenic mechanism in cognitive decline and neurodegenerative diseases[7][47]. Aerobic exercise reduces circulating levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as interleukin-6 (IL-6) and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), thereby creating a more neuroprotective neurochemical environment[47]. Additionally, aerobic exercise enhances autonomic balance by reducing sympathetic nervous system dominance and promoting parasympathetic activation, leading to decreased cortisol secretion and improved stress resilience[7][20].

### Neurobiological Pathways of Resistance Training

Resistance training operates through partially overlapping yet mechanistically distinct pathways compared to aerobic exercise[1][15][20]. While both modalities enhance cerebral blood flow and promote neurogenesis, resistance training shows particularly robust effects on insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-1), a neurotrophic factor with powerful effects on neuronal survival, synaptic plasticity, and cognitive function[1][20]. The mechanical loading imposed by resistance exercise—the force exerted against muscles—triggers upstream activation of signaling cascades that culminate in elevated plasma IGF-1 levels[1]. Unlike aerobic exercise, which primarily stimulates BDNF, resistance training's preferential elevation of IGF-1 represents a distinct mechanism of cognitive enhancement[19].

Research comparing these modalities in animal models and human studies reveals that resistance training exerts selective effects on gray matter density in regions governing cognitive control and attention[1][30]. Moderate-to-high-intensity resistance training acutely enhances cognitive performance within a narrow temporal window—approximately 10-20 minutes post-exercise—likely reflecting the elevated cerebral blood flow and acute neurotrophic factor release occurring during this period[1][1]. The acute cognitive benefits of resistance training appear particularly pronounced for tasks requiring inhibitory control and conflict resolution, domains dependent on prefrontal cortex function[1][30].

Chronic resistance training effects involve substantial structural remodeling of brain networks supporting executive function[1][13]. Progressive resistance training over 12-24 weeks increases gray matter volume in the prefrontal cortex and improves functional connectivity within executive control networks[1][30][45]. These structural adaptations appear mediated partly by resistance training-induced improvements in cardiovascular fitness and muscle-derived myokines—signaling molecules released from contracting muscle tissue that cross the blood-brain barrier and modulate neural function[1][20]. Additionally, resistance training reduces systemic inflammation through similar mechanisms as aerobic exercise, though the magnitude of inflammatory reduction may differ between modalities[1][47].

## Cognitive Performance: Comparative Effects of Running and Resistance Training

### Aerobic Exercise and Memory Function

Recent meta-analyses and systematic reviews from 2024-2025 demonstrate that aerobic exercise, particularly running and walking, provides the most robust enhancement of memory-dependent cognitive functions[1][4][13][18][48][13]. Among multiple cognitive domains examined through meta-analytic techniques combining data from dozens of randomized controlled trials, aerobic exercise emerges as the superior modality for memory enhancement across healthy aging populations and those with mild cognitive impairment[13][13]. The standardized mean difference for memory improvements following aerobic exercise protocols reaches approximately 0.42, indicating small-to-moderate effect sizes when aggregating evidence across diverse study populations[13][13].

The mechanisms underlying aerobic exercise's selective memory benefits relate fundamentally to its pronounced effects on hippocampal structure and function[1][45]. Longitudinal neuroimaging studies reveal that aerobic exercise programs spanning 6-12 months increase hippocampal volume, particularly in anterior hippocampal regions critical for spatial memory and contextual learning[1][45]. This volumetric expansion correlates directly with improvements in memory test performance, suggesting that structural brain changes represent the neural substrate of cognitive enhancement[1][45]. Additionally, aerobic exercise-induced elevations in BDNF specifically support synaptic plasticity mechanisms within hippocampal circuits, facilitating the encoding and consolidation of new memories[1][20].

For individuals with mild cognitive impairment, a preclinical state with high conversion rates to Alzheimer's disease dementia, aerobic exercise demonstrates particularly strong cognitive benefits[18][48]. Meta-analytic findings show that moderate-intensity aerobic exercise conducted four times weekly for 50 minutes or longer produces substantial improvements in global cognitive function, with standardized mean differences exceeding 0.81[18][48]. These benefits translate to meaningful real-world improvements in ability to remember daily events, navigate environments, and maintain independence in activities of daily living[18][48][18]. The memory improvements from aerobic exercise persist beyond the intervention period in some studies, suggesting that exercise-induced neuroplastic changes establish resilience against cognitive decline[18][48].

### Resistance Training and Executive Function with Selective Memory Benefits

While aerobic exercise shows primary efficacy for memory, resistance training demonstrates greater effectiveness for executive functions—the higher-order cognitive processes including inhibitory control, task-switching, and working memory[1][13][30][13]. A comprehensive network meta-analysis of exercise effects on cognition in 2024 ranked resistance training as the most effective modality for global cognitive function (standardized mean difference = 0.55) and inhibitory control (standardized mean difference = 0.31, with 82.1% probability of being optimal)[13][13]. These findings suggest that resistance training's unique effects on prefrontal cortex systems supporting cognitive control provide domain-specific advantages for executive function[1][13].

The acute cognitive effects of resistance training deserve particular emphasis given their rapid onset and potential practical applications[1]. Single sessions of moderate-to-high-intensity resistance exercise (70-80% of one repetition maximum) enhance visuospatial processing and executive function performance when tested 10-20 minutes post-exercise[1][1]. This acute effect window reflects the immediate elevation of cerebral blood flow and neurotrophic factor release during the early post-exercise period[1][1]. For practical applications, this suggests that resistance training could be strategically timed prior to cognitively demanding tasks to maximize performance[1].

Recent evidence also reveals selective benefits of resistance training on memory, though distinct from aerobic exercise patterns[1][15]. Meta-analytic aggregation of 17 randomized controlled trials examining resistance training effects reveals significant improvements in working memory (standardized mean difference = 0.44), verbal learning and memory (standardized mean difference = 0.44 with absolute difference of 3.01 points on test scores), and spatial memory span (standardized mean difference = 0.63)[15]. These improvements rival those observed with aerobic exercise for certain memory domains, suggesting complementary rather than strictly competitive effects[1][13][15]. The differential effectiveness for specific memory domains—with aerobic exercise superior for hippocampal-dependent episodic memory and resistance training stronger for working memory—points to distinct neural substrates engaged by each modality[1][15].

### Optimal Protocols for Cognitive Enhancement

The question of optimal exercise prescription parameters—frequency, intensity, duration, and type—has been clarified substantially by recent research[1][4][13][25][48][13]. For aerobic exercise and running, moderate intensity appears optimal, with intensities ranging from 50-70% of maximum heart rate producing superior cognitive benefits compared to low or high intensity[1][4][25]. Recent meta-analyses indicate that moderate-intensity aerobic exercise performed 3-7 times weekly in sessions lasting 20-45 minutes yields optimal benefits for executive functions[1][4]. For individuals with mild cognitive impairment specifically, four weekly sessions of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise lasting at least 50 minutes produced the most pronounced cognitive improvements[18][48][18]. This finding suggests that cognitive impairment may necessitate higher exercise volume for optimal therapeutic benefit[18][48].

Resistance training protocols demonstrating the most robust cognitive benefits follow different parameters[1][13][15]. Progressive resistance training at moderate-to-high intensity (40-80% of one repetition maximum) performed twice weekly appears particularly beneficial for memory and attentional processes[1]. For global cognitive function, resistance training protocols involving sessions of approximately 45 minutes duration performed twice weekly over 12-week periods produced effect sizes of approximately 0.55, representing the largest effect for this modality[13]. Notably, the twice-weekly frequency for resistance training contrasts with the higher frequencies optimal for aerobic exercise, potentially reflecting distinct physiological adaptation timelines[1][13][15].

## Sleep Quality: Evidence for Running and Resistance Training

### Aerobic Exercise Effects on Sleep Architecture and Quality

Recent systematic reviews and meta-analyses examining aerobic exercise effects on sleep in 2024-2025 document significant improvements in sleep quality among older adults with sleep disturbances[7][47][7]. The aggregate evidence indicates that aerobic exercise reduces Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index scores by approximately 0.98 standard deviations, a magnitude consistent with small-to-moderate clinical benefit[7][7]. Beyond summary metrics, aerobic exercise influences sleep architecture—the organized progression through distinct sleep stages—in ways that support cognitive and restorative functions[7][8][11].

Aerobic exercise enhances melatonin secretion, the primary circadian rhythm regulator that establishes the temporal organization of sleep-wake cycles[7][7]. By promoting more consistent and appropriately timed melatonin release, aerobic exercise advances sleep onset and stabilizes the sleep-wake cycle, particularly beneficial for individuals with circadian misalignment common in aging[7][7]. Additionally, aerobic exercise promotes parasympathetic nervous system dominance and reduces sympathetic tone, physiology conducive to the relaxation essential for sleep initiation[7][7]. The exercise-induced elevation of parasympathetic activity, reflected in increased heart rate variability and vagal tone, facilitates the transition into sleep and reduces nocturnal awakenings[7][7].

From a neurochemical perspective, aerobic exercise increases gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) activity and elevates serotonin levels, neurotransmitter changes that collectively promote deeper, more restorative sleep stages[7][7]. The improvement in sleep efficiency—the percentage of time spent asleep while in bed—represents a particularly important metric for older adults, among whom fragmented and interrupted sleep is prevalent[7][9][7]. Evidence indicates that both aerobic-only exercise groups and combined aerobic and resistance training groups demonstrate improved sleep efficiency, though sleep duration gains vary by modality[9][47].

The optimal aerobic exercise prescription for sleep enhancement involves specific parameters that have been identified through network meta-analysis and systematic review[7][36][39][7]. Aerobic exercise performed more than three times weekly produces superior sleep benefits compared to lower frequencies, with optimal effects observed at 3-5 weekly sessions[7][36][7]. Session duration demonstrates a curvilinear relationship with sleep benefits—sessions lasting 60 minutes or longer produce greater sleep quality improvements than shorter sessions, though excessively long sessions may produce fatigue that disrupts subsequent sleep[7][36][7]. An intervention duration of 12 weeks represents an optimal timeframe, sufficient to establish consistent behavioral and physiological adaptations while maintaining adherence and preventing intervention fatigue[7][7].

Notably, moderate-intensity aerobic exercise appears more effective for sleep improvement than high-intensity efforts[10][47]. This finding contrasts with some cognitive domains where high-intensity exercise produces superior effects, suggesting that sleep quality optimization may require different physiological demands than executive function enhancement[10][47][7]. Moderate-intensity aerobic exercise lasting approximately 45 minutes, conducted four to five times weekly for 12-16 weeks, emerges as an optimized prescription based on systematic analysis of available evidence[7][7].

### Resistance Training Advantages for Sleep Duration and Efficiency

While aerobic exercise produces meaningful sleep improvements, recent evidence reveals that resistance training demonstrates particularly pronounced effects on sleep duration and efficiency[2][9][12]. A landmark study presented at the American Heart Association conference compared sleep outcomes across exercise modalities in sedentary adults with high blood pressure and sleep complaints[9]. The resistance training group, engaging in 60-minute sessions three times weekly using weight machines, achieved the most impressive sleep gains, with participants reporting an additional 40 minutes of sleep per night[9]. This substantially exceeded gains in the aerobic-only group (23 minutes additional sleep nightly) and combined exercise group (17 minutes additional sleep nightly)[9]. Critically, only the resistance training and combined exercise groups demonstrated improvements in sleep efficiency, suggesting that resistance training influences sleep architecture differently than aerobic exercise alone[9].

A 12-week randomized controlled trial in older women further documented resistance training's sleep benefits, revealing significant reductions in Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index scores among both poor-sleep and good-sleep subgroups[2]. The study measured objective cognitive function, functional capacity, mental health, and sleep quality across multiple validated instruments[2]. Results demonstrated that resistance training improved subjective sleep quality regardless of baseline sleep status, with Beck Anxiety Inventory scores declining substantially in both resistance training groups[2]. The magnitude of improvement suggests that resistance training may provide broader psychosocial benefits affecting sleep quality beyond pure physiological mechanisms[2].

The mechanisms through which resistance training enhances sleep differ partially from those of aerobic exercise[2][9][12]. Resistance training increases neuromuscular demand and muscle damage requiring repair, physiological processes that necessitate deep sleep stages (slow-wave sleep, or N3) and rapid eye movement sleep for efficient muscle recovery and memory consolidation[2][38]. The growth hormone release during deep sleep, enhanced by resistance training-induced muscle tissue remodeling needs, may explain resistance training's particular effectiveness for sleep duration[2][38]. Additionally, resistance training reduces systemic inflammation through elevations in anti-inflammatory myokines released from contracting muscle, creating conditions favorable for uninterrupted, restorative sleep[2][12].

Meta-analytic evidence examining exercise modalities' effects on sleep quality through network meta-analysis reveals Pilates as most effective (ranked 91.7%), followed by aerobic exercise (69.7%), combined aerobic and resistance training (59.4%), and resistance training alone (58.6%)[36][39]. However, these rankings must be interpreted cautiously given that optimal doses vary substantially by modality[36][39]. The overall optimal exercise dose identified through Bayesian dose-response analysis is approximately 920 metabolic equivalent minutes per week, with a nonlinear U-shaped relationship between dose and sleep benefits[36][39]. This U-shaped relationship indicates that both insufficient and excessive exercise may produce suboptimal sleep improvements, with moderate exercise volumes yielding maximal benefits[36][39][47].

## Comparative Efficacy and Synergistic Effects of Combined Training

### Network Meta-Analytic Findings on Exercise Modality Comparison

The most comprehensive comparative evidence emerges from network meta-analyses that simultaneously evaluate multiple exercise modalities using sophisticated statistical techniques enabling direct and indirect comparisons[13][13]. A 2024 network meta-analysis including 58 randomized controlled trials and 4,349 healthy older adults aged 60 and above provides unprecedented clarity on comparative efficacy[13][13]. Results demonstrate that different exercise modalities produce domain-specific cognitive benefits rather than uniformly superior effects across all domains[13][13].

For global cognitive function, mind-body exercise (including yoga and tai chi) demonstrated the largest effect (standardized mean difference = 0.62), followed by resistance training (standardized mean difference = 0.55) and aerobic exercise (standardized mean difference = 0.49)[13][13]. However, when examining specific cognitive domains, the ranking changes substantially[13][13]. For memory function, aerobic exercise ranks as most effective, while resistance training demonstrates superior efficacy for inhibitory control and executive function[13][13]. These domain-specific differences reflect the distinct neurobiological mechanisms each modality engages, with aerobic exercise's superior memory effects reflecting its pronounced hippocampal actions and resistance training's executive function advantages reflecting prefrontal cortex engagement[1][13][13].

The clinical implications of these findings suggest that exercise prescription should be personalized based on individual cognitive priorities[13][13]. For individuals concerned primarily with memory preservation or at risk for Alzheimer's disease dementia characterized by early hippocampal pathology, aerobic exercise recommendations appear most strongly supported by evidence[13][13]. Conversely, for those exhibiting executive dysfunction or seeking to optimize attention and cognitive control, resistance training emerges as the preferential modality[13][13]. However, most comprehensive exercise interventions combine modalities to achieve broad-based cognitive benefit[1][13].

### Combined and Concurrent Training Approaches

The effects of concurrent resistance and aerobic training on cognitive function reveal complex patterns suggesting that combination approaches offer advantages under specific circumstances[1][34][42][1]. While some studies examining concurrent training without cognitive engagement report minimal additional benefits beyond single-modality training, dual-task training—combining high-intensity concurrent exercise with concurrent cognitive tasks—demonstrates superior efficacy in enhancing executive function and processing speed[1][34]. For instance, dance and tai chi, which inherently combine physical movement with cognitive demands such as balance, coordination, and sequential memory, produce particularly large cognitive benefits[1][34][13].

A systematic review of 50 published intervention studies involving 6,164 healthy older adults examined combined cognitive-physical training compared to single-domain interventions[34]. Results indicated that combined training produced small advantages for executive function compared to cognitive training alone, while achieving similar effects to cognitive training for attention, memory, language, processing speed, and global cognition[34]. However, combined training achieved higher gains in balance than single physical training, suggesting transfer effects wherein cognitive training improves motor coordination[34]. Most notably, simultaneously combining physical and cognitive demands produced larger benefits than sequential or separate training[34], indicating that the brain's simultaneous engagement in physical and cognitive challenge amplifies neuroplastic responses[34].

A network meta-analysis examining cognitive-physical combined interventions with varying numbers of exercise components found that interventions incorporating three or more exercise components produced the largest memory improvements[31]. Meta-analytic effect sizes for memory improved from 0.26 (with 2 components) to 0.43 (with 3+ components) compared to controls[31]. For executive function, single-component interventions showed highest efficacy, suggesting potential optimization at different levels of complexity for different cognitive domains[31].

## Sleep-Cognition Interactions and Bidirectional Relationships

### Sleep as a Mediator of Exercise-Induced Cognitive Benefits

Recent evidence increasingly documents bidirectional relationships between sleep quality and cognitive function, suggesting that exercise-induced sleep improvements may partially mediate cognitive enhancements[8][21][35]. A study examining the role of sleep on cognitive performance in ultra-endurance athletes revealed complex relationships wherein accumulated sleep prior to cognitive testing paradoxically associated with poorer performance on the Digit Symbol Substitution Task[35]. However, adequate pre-event sleep predicted better reaction time and transfer rate performance[35], indicating nonlinear and context-dependent sleep-cognition relationships[35].

In older adults, the relationship between sleep architecture changes and cognitive decline demonstrated that changes in non-REM sleep stage N2 associated with cognitive decline on the Trail Making Test, while changes in rapid eye movement (REM) sleep duration showed no significant associations[8]. This selective association of N2 sleep with cognition suggests that exercise-induced changes in specific sleep stages, rather than total sleep time alone, may represent critical mechanisms through which physical activity protects cognitive function[8]. Furthermore, individuals showing lower increases in light stage (N1) sleep over time experienced less cognitive decline, indicating that preserving deeper sleep stages constitutes an important neuroprotective mechanism[8].

Physical activity appears to partially compensate for cognitive deficits related to poor sleep quality[21]. A recent review noted that while cognitive function is compromised by sleep restriction, physically active individuals demonstrate superior cognitive performance despite sleep disturbances[21]. This suggests that exercise activates compensatory cognitive mechanisms or protective neural systems that buffer against sleep-related cognitive decline[21]. For older adults with mild cognitive impairment, sleep disturbance prevalence reaches 60-70%, substantially higher than the 35-37% observed in cognitively healthy peers[18][48][18], indicating that optimizing both sleep and exercise represents a critical dual-target intervention strategy[18][48].

### Acute Exercise Timing and Sleep Architecture Modification

The timing of exercise relative to sleep substantially influences both acute cognitive benefits and sleep architecture[27][38][39]. Morning exercise produces differential effects depending on chronotype—the individual's intrinsic circadian timing preference[24][27]. In a school-based study examining exercise timing effects, morning physical activity produced the greatest benefits for attention and working memory, particularly in early chronotypes (morning-preferring individuals)[27]. However, late chronotypes demonstrated attenuated benefits from early morning exercise, suggesting circadian misalignment reduces exercise efficacy[27]. These findings indicate that personalized exercise timing based on individual circadian biology may optimize both cognitive and sleep-related benefits[27][38].

Evening exercise timing presents considerations for sleep quality, as high-intensity exercise performed close to bedtime may elevate core body temperature and sympathetic arousal, potentially disrupting sleep onset[38][39]. However, evidence indicates that moderate-intensity evening exercise conducted 2-4 hours before bedtime produces beneficial sleep effects without sleep disruption[38][39]. The typical exercise-induced increase in core body temperature during activity, followed by a compensatory decrease in the subsequent 30-90 minutes, facilitates sleep initiation through the thermoregulatory mechanisms underlying circadian sleep pressure[7][10][38].

## Sex Differences and Population-Specific Responses

### Sex Disparities in Exercise Effects on Cognition and Sleep

Biological sex represents an important moderator of exercise effects on cognition and sleep, with recent evidence revealing systematic differences in response magnitudes between men and women[37][40]. Women aged 55 years and older demonstrate greater cognitive benefits from aerobic training compared to men, with meta-analytical evidence revealing larger effect sizes in studies with higher female participation (effect sizes: 2.83 for studies ≥71% female vs. 1.34 for studies <71% female for executive function)[37]. This sex difference emerges particularly for executive function improvements, where women show more robust gains than men following identical aerobic training protocols[37].

The neurobiological basis for sex differences involves differential hormonal profiles and neuroplastic signaling pathways[40]. Women's hippocampal neurogenesis following novel learning tasks demonstrates greater response compared to men, particularly during ovulatory phases of the menstrual cycle[37]. Postmenopausal women experience accelerated hippocampal volume loss due to estrogen deficiency, potentially explaining why aerobic exercise—with its pronounced hippocampal effects—provides particularly strong cognitive protection in this population[40]. In Alzheimer's disease specifically, women exhibit faster cognitive decline and greater disease progression compared to men, a sex-dependent vulnerability potentially mitigated more effectively by aerobic exercise in women[40].

Resistance training effects show more sex-equitable patterns, with both men and women demonstrating comparable improvements in strength and associated cognitive benefits[37][40]. However, for depression alleviation—a key neuropsychiatric symptom in both normal aging and dementia—women demonstrate greater responsiveness to resistance training compared to men[40]. Six months of progressive resistance training produced a 24% decrease in depressive symptoms in university women with greater effect magnitudes than observed in comparative male samples[40].

Sex differences in sleep response to exercise are less well-characterized, though preliminary evidence suggests women may experience greater sleep efficiency improvements with resistance training compared to men[2][9]. A randomized controlled trial in older women revealed substantial sleep quality improvements across cognitive and sleep measures following 12 weeks of resistance training[2]. Whether comparable benefits occur in men requires additional investigation, as many sleep studies remain predominantly female in composition[2][9].

### Age-Related Variation in Exercise Response

Age represents another critical moderator, with research indicating that individuals aged 65-75 years demonstrate the largest cognitive benefits from exercise interventions[13][13]. Both aerobic and resistance training show peak efficacy in this age window, with effect sizes substantially exceeding those observed in younger or older cohorts[13][13]. This apparent "sweet spot" age range may reflect the balance between cognitive reserve capacity and age-related decline prevalence, creating optimal conditions for exercise-induced neuroplastic adaptation[13][13].

Older adults over 80 years show more modest cognitive gains from exercise, potentially reflecting accumulated neurodegeneration and reduced neuroplastic capacity at advanced ages[13][13]. However, qualitative improvements in functional independence, mobility, and quality of life—outcomes not captured by cognitive testing alone—remain substantial even in very advanced ages[13][13]. For mild cognitive impairment populations specifically, older-old adults (80+ years) demonstrate preserved responsiveness to exercise interventions, suggesting that cognitive impairment status rather than chronological age determines exercise efficacy[13][13].

Sleep response to exercise varies with age, with older adults typically requiring longer intervention periods (12+ weeks) to achieve maximal sleep improvements compared to younger adults[7][36][39]. However, the magnitude of sleep improvement in older adults often exceeds that in younger populations, reflecting greater baseline sleep disturbance in aging[7][36][7].

## Practical Implementation and Exercise Prescription Guidelines

### Evidence-Based Exercise Prescriptions for Cognitive Enhancement

Synthesizing recent evidence permits formulation of evidence-based exercise prescriptions optimized for cognitive enhancement[1][4][13][15][25][48][13]. For individuals prioritizing memory preservation and hippocampal health, moderate-intensity aerobic exercise (running, brisk walking, or cycling at 50-70% of maximum heart rate) conducted 3-7 times weekly in 20-45 minute sessions represents an optimal prescription[1][4]. For more sedentary individuals or those with existing cognitive impairment, starting with 3-4 weekly sessions of 30-40 minutes at a pace permitting conversation (moderate intensity) and progressively increasing frequency and duration over 4-6 weeks proves practical and sustainable[1][4][18][48].

For global cognitive function and executive function enhancement, progressive resistance training at 40-80% of one repetition maximum performed twice weekly in 45-minute sessions over 12-week cycles produces robust benefits[1][13][15]. A practical protocol involves 8-12 repetitions per set across 2-3 sets for major muscle groups (legs, chest, back, shoulders, arms) with 1-2 minutes rest between sets[2][15]. For individuals new to resistance training, starting with lighter loads (20-30% of estimated one repetition maximum) and emphasizing proper form over load intensity proves more sustainable and reduces injury risk[1][15].

For combined cognitive and sleep benefits, concurrent training integrating aerobic and resistance components appears optimal, particularly when delivered through modalities requiring cognitive engagement such as dance, tai chi, or circuit training combining choreography with strength movements[1][34][13]. An evidence-based concurrent protocol might include 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity weekly (distributed across 3-5 sessions) plus two 45-minute resistance training sessions, potentially combined through circuit training integrating both modalities sequentially within single sessions[1][34].

### Sleep-Optimized Exercise Prescriptions

For individuals primarily concerned with sleep quality improvement, recent meta-analytic evidence supports a specific exercise prescription[7][36][39][7]. Aerobic exercise performed 3-5 times weekly in sessions lasting 60+ minutes at moderate intensity (perceivable exertion permitting conversation but not complete comfort) for 12-week intervention periods produces substantial sleep quality improvements[7][36][39][7]. Notably, a U-shaped dose-response relationship indicates that 920 metabolic equivalent minutes per week represents an optimal exercise volume, with both insufficient and excessive exercise producing suboptimal sleep benefits[36][39].

For resistance training specifically targeting sleep improvements, two-to-three weekly sessions of 45-60 minutes focusing on compound movements (squats, deadlifts, presses, rows) performed at moderate-to-heavy loads (60-80% of one repetition maximum) yields the largest sleep duration and efficiency gains[2][9]. The 40-minute nightly sleep gain documented with resistance training far exceeds improvements from other modalities, making it a particularly valuable intervention for individuals with chronic sleep restriction[9].

Timing considerations include preference for morning or early afternoon exercise when possible, reserving evening exercise for 2-4 hours before bedtime to avoid thermoregulatory and sympathetic nervous system activation immediately preceding sleep[7][38][39]. Consistency and regularity prove essential—maintaining stable exercise timing appears to amplify circadian rhythm entrainment and sleep architecture improvements[7][24][27].

## Recent Research Developments and Emerging Evidence

### Brain Age Research and Long-Term Cognitive Reserve

Compelling recent findings from 2024-2025 employ novel neuroimaging biomarkers quantifying biological brain age—how old the brain appears on magnetic resonance imaging relative to chronological age[26]. A randomized clinical trial of 130 healthy adults aged 26-58 revealed that 12 months of moderate-to-vigorous aerobic exercise reduced brain predicted age difference by approximately 0.6 years, indicating a younger-appearing brain. The control group showed a slight increase in brain age of 0.35 years, resulting in a nearly one-year between-group difference in brain aging trajectory. Remarkably, participants aged 30-50 showed particularly robust brain age reductions, suggesting that early-to-midlife exercise provides powerful preventive effects.

These brain age findings acquire additional significance given longitudinal evidence that brain-predicted age difference predicts mortality risk, cognitive decline, and dementia incidence. A six-month high-intensity interval training (HIIT) study documented that only HIIT participants showed positive changes in hippocampal functioning on repeat testing, with benefits persisting at five-year follow-up despite discontinued training[16]. This persistence suggests that exercise-induced neuroplastic changes establish lasting resilience, potentially delaying or preventing age-related cognitive decline across decades[16].

### High-Intensity Interval Training as an Emerging Cognitive Enhancement Modality

While traditional aerobic exercise at moderate intensity dominates evidence for cognitive benefits, emerging research on high-intensity interval training (HIIT) suggests potential advantages for certain cognitive domains and populations[14][16][25]. HIIT involves repeated cycles of short-duration high-intensity effort (80-95% maximum heart rate) followed by lower-intensity recovery periods[16]. Meta-analytic evidence from 2024 indicates that HIIT significantly enhances information processing speed (standardized mean difference = 0.33), executive function (standardized mean difference = 0.38), and memory (standardized mean difference = 0.21)[14]. Subgroup analyses reveal that HIIT demonstrates particular efficacy for information processing in adults aged 60 and above, improved executive functioning across all ages, and enhanced memory in adults aged 30-60[14].

For practical implementation, HIIT protocols lasting less than 8 weeks enhanced executive function and memory, while longer interventions exceeding 8 weeks showed improvements across all cognitive domains[14]. This rapid efficacy timeline makes HIIT attractive for individuals with limited time availability, though sustainability concerns may limit long-term adherence for some populations[14][16]. The cognitive benefits from HIIT appear mediated through similar mechanisms as moderate-intensity aerobic exercise, including cerebral blood flow increases and neurotrophic factor elevation, but potentially achieved more efficiently through greater cardiovascular stimulus[14][16].

## Recommendations and Future Research Directions

### Clinical Practice Recommendations

Healthcare providers and exercise specialists working with individuals seeking cognitive enhancement and improved sleep should implement personalized exercise prescriptions based on individual preferences, baseline fitness, comorbidities, and specific cognitive or sleep goals[1][3][13][15][48][13]. For memory preservation and hippocampal health in cognitively normal aging, moderate-intensity aerobic exercise of 150-300 minutes weekly represents a well-established, guideline-concordant recommendation[1][3]. For individuals with mild cognitive impairment, particularly those with memory complaints, similar or slightly higher aerobic exercise volumes (approaching the upper range) prove especially beneficial[18][48][18].

For executive function and global cognitive health prioritization, progressive resistance training twice to three times weekly provides complementary benefits to aerobic exercise and should be incorporated into comprehensive cognitive health strategies[1][13][15]. The combination of aerobic and resistance training appears to provide the broadest cognitive benefits, approaching or exceeding any single modality for comprehensive brain health[1][34][1]. Implementation should emphasize behavioral sustainability through progressive goal-setting, role-modeling, and social support, as sustained long-term adherence—not just exercise initiation—determines health outcomes[46].

For sleep complaints specifically, resistance training emerges as a particularly potent intervention based on recent evidence[2][9][12], yet aerobic exercise remains an evidence-supported first-line recommendation given the dual cognitive and sleep benefits[7][7]. Combining both modalities or selecting resistance training for isolated sleep problems while maintaining aerobic exercise for cognitive reasons represents a comprehensive approach[2][9][34].

### Critical Research Gaps and Future Investigation

Despite substantial recent advances, important research questions remain inadequately addressed. The mechanisms through which exercise-induced brain age reduction translates to prevented dementia incidence require long-term prospective study. Do individuals showing greater brain age reductions with exercise demonstrate lower subsequent dementia incidence, and what exercise parameters most robustly predict neuroprotection? Mechanistic research employing advanced neuroimaging, cerebrospinal fluid biomarkers (amyloid-beta, phosphorylated tau), and molecular profiling could identify the specific exercise-induced changes most critical for neurodegeneration prevention.

Sex differences in exercise response require substantially greater investigation, particularly regarding resistance training effects on sleep and cognition in men compared to women[37][40]. Most published sleep research disproportionately enrolls women, limiting generalizability[2][9][12]. Future research should mandate sex-stratified analysis and employ larger male participant samples to clarify whether observed sex differences reflect true biological variation or enrollment artifacts[37][40].

The mechanisms through which exercise-induced sleep improvements mediate cognitive enhancements versus operating as independent outcomes remain incompletely characterized[8][21]. Longitudinal studies with concurrent sleep polysomnography, cognitive testing, and neuroimaging would clarify whether specific sleep architecture changes (e.g., increased slow-wave sleep percentage or REM duration) represent critical intermediate pathways through which exercise enhances cognition[8][35].

Finally, implementation science research addressing adherence determinants and sustainability represents an urgent priority[46]. While exercise interventions demonstrate efficacy in controlled research settings, real-world adherence often lags, undermining public health impact[46]. Future research employing behavioral economics, digital technologies, and community-based participatory approaches should identify cost-effective strategies for promoting sustained exercise engagement in diverse populations[46].

## Conclusion

Recent evidence from 2024-2025 demonstrates that both running (aerobic exercise) and resistance training provide significant cognitive and sleep benefits, operating through distinct neurobiological mechanisms and with domain-specific advantages. Aerobic exercise, particularly moderate-intensity running and walking conducted 3-7 times weekly, produces superior memory enhancement and hippocampal health through BDNF elevation and exercise-induced neurogenesis[1][13][13]. The hippocampal volume increases and improved memory consolidation resulting from consistent aerobic exercise represent particularly robust and functionally meaningful cognitive benefits[1][45]. Resistance training demonstrates greater efficacy for global cognition, inhibitory control, and executive function through IGF-1 elevation and prefrontal cortex engagement[1][13][30][13].

For sleep quality, resistance training emerges as a particularly powerful intervention, with participants gaining approximately 40 minutes nightly of sleep following 12 weeks of progressive resistance training[9]. This substantial sleep gain substantially exceeds improvements from aerobic exercise alone or combined approaches[9]. Aerobic exercise provides more modest but still meaningful sleep benefits (approximately 23 minutes nightly) while simultaneously delivering robust cognitive benefits, making it an efficient dual-purpose intervention[9][7].

The optimal approach for maximizing both cognitive performance and sleep quality appears to involve combined aerobic and resistance training, with specific protocols personalizing intensity, frequency, and duration based on individual goals, age, baseline fitness, cognitive status, and sex[1][13][15][34][13]. For memory and cognitive reserve in early-to-midlife ages (30-50 years), aerobic exercise receives particularly strong evidence and may provide preventive benefits protecting brain health across decades. For sleep quality optimization, resistance training demonstrates clear superiority[2][9]. For comprehensive brain health addressing multiple cognitive domains, integrated programs combining aerobic exercise at moderate intensity with progressive resistance training appear optimal[1][34][1].

As global populations age and cognitive decline emerges as a leading public health concern, evidence-based, accessible exercise interventions represent perhaps the most potent non-pharmacological tools available to delay dementia onset and maintain cognitive reserve across the lifespan[1][3]. The convergence of recent findings from neuroimaging, meta-analytic synthesis, and mechanistic investigations establishes compelling support for exercise as foundational to cognitive health maintenance and sleep quality improvement, with specific modality, intensity, and frequency recommendations now sufficiently refined to guide clinical practice and public health recommendations. Future research employing long-term follow-up, diverse populations, and advanced mechanistic investigation will further clarify optimal exercise prescriptions and identify which individuals benefit most from specific modalities, enabling truly personalized approaches to brain health throughout adulthood.
