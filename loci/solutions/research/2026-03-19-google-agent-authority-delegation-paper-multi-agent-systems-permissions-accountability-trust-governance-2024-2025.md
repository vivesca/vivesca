# Authority Delegation in Multi-Agent AI Systems: Frameworks for Permissions, Accountability, and Trust Governance

The emergence of autonomous AI agents capable of orchestrating complex workflows across distributed systems has fundamentally transformed how organizations must approach authority delegation, permission management, and governance. Unlike traditional software systems where control flows remain largely predictable and confined within predetermined boundaries, agentic AI systems introduce a new paradigm where autonomous actors make decisions, delegate tasks to other agents, and accumulate permissions across interconnected networks in ways that static access control models were never designed to accommodate. This research report synthesizes the latest advances in intelligent delegation frameworks, trust calibration mechanisms, and governance architectures that are reshaping how organizations can safely deploy autonomous agent systems at scale. Drawing on recent foundational research from Google DeepMind, OpenID Foundation, emerging standards protocols, and enterprise security practitioners, this report presents a comprehensive examination of how authority delegation in multi-agent systems can be engineered to balance operational autonomy with meaningful accountability, dynamic trust assessment with security assurance, and scalability with governance rigor.

## The Evolution of Agent Authority Delegation: From Traditional Delegation to Agentic Ecosystems

### Historical Context and the Authority Delegation Problem

Authority delegation has long been a fundamental challenge in organizational systems, extending far beyond the domain of software and technology. In traditional human organizations, delegation involves the transfer of responsibility, authority, and accountability from a principal (typically a manager) to an agent (typically a subordinate) under explicit constraints regarding scope, authority, and expected outcomes. The classical principal-agent problem, formalized in economic theory, identifies the core tension at the heart of all delegation: how can a principal ensure that an agent acts in the principal's interest when the agent has divergent goals, better information about their own actions, and potential incentives to shirk effort or act opportunistically[1][6]. For centuries, organizations have managed this challenge through hierarchical structures, explicit contracts, monitoring systems, reputation mechanisms, and consequence frameworks. However, the rise of autonomous AI agents introduces unprecedented complexity to this problem because agents now operate at machine speed, can execute hundreds of decisions autonomously, interact with other agents in unpredictable ways, and accumulate permissions across systems in ways that are often invisible to their human principals.

Traditional software systems approached this challenge through static access control models like role-based access control (RBAC) or attribute-based access control (ABAC), where permissions are granted at deployment time and remain static until explicitly modified. These systems assume that the software components in question operate under relatively predictable conditions, follow predetermined workflows, and do not make autonomous decisions about what actions to take or which other systems to interact with. However, agentic AI fundamentally violates these assumptions. An agent tasked with optimizing supply chain operations might discover that it needs access to supplier databases, financial systems, and third-party APIs to fulfill its objective effectively. An agent operating in a customer service context might delegate portions of complex requests to specialized sub-agents, creating recursive delegation chains where authority must be progressively attenuated at each hop while remaining verifiable at every step. An agent operating in a financial trading context might accumulate permissions across multiple asset classes, risk management systems, and execution platforms in ways that create systemic risk if not carefully bounded[12][21].

### The Emergence of Intelligent Delegation as a Core Requirement

Recent research from Google DeepMind has reframed delegation from a simple technical problem to a multidimensional sociotechnical challenge requiring integration of economic theory, cryptographic verification, policy enforcement, and human oversight[1][6]. The core insight is that existing task decomposition methods treat delegation merely as breaking a complex problem into sub-steps, while truly safe and scalable delegation requires explicit transfer of authority, assignment of responsibility, establishment of accountability mechanisms, and ongoing trust calibration. As the research community has increasingly recognized, moving from isolated chatbots to an interconnected "agentic web" of interacting systems requires protocols that go far beyond simple prompt engineering or heuristic-based orchestration[1][6][9].

The timeframe for this transition is compressed. Industry projections suggest that most large enterprises will have AI agents embedded in production workflows within twelve to eighteen months, yet many organizations have not yet established the governance frameworks necessary to manage these autonomous systems safely[7]. This creates a critical window where early adopters who invest in intelligent delegation frameworks and robust governance architectures will define operational standards and competitive advantages, while late movers will inherit technical debt that compounds exponentially as agent deployments scale[7][8].

## Foundational Principles of Authority Delegation in Multi-Agent Systems

### Redefining Authority in the Context of Autonomous Agents

Authority in the context of agentic AI systems must be understood as multidimensional: it encompasses not only what an agent is permitted to do (the scope of actions), but also under what conditions those actions are permitted (the context constraints), who granted the authority (the source of legitimacy), on whose behalf the authority is being exercised (the principal relationship), and what mechanisms exist to verify that authority is being exercised appropriately (the verification framework)[1][2][10]. A research agent in a financial services context, for example, might be authorized to access equity price data, but only during market hours, only for assets in a predetermined watchlist, only on behalf of a specific human analyst, and only with real-time logging and anomaly detection monitoring[21][23][26].

This multidimensional conception of authority requires equally multidimensional frameworks for its management. The traditional binary "allowed or denied" authorization model proves inadequate because it cannot capture the nuanced trade-offs between operational efficiency and safety that autonomous agents require[10][18]. A junior-level agent might be authorized to recommend actions but not execute them, requiring explicit human approval before proceeding[10][18]. A senior-level agent might be authorized to execute routine actions but must escalate edge cases and exceptions to human judgment[10][18]. A principal-level agent might operate autonomously within carefully defined boundaries but must cease all action and escalate immediately if it detects patterns that deviate significantly from expected behavior[10]. This hierarchical progression of autonomy levels, properly implemented with explicit criteria for advancement and continuous monitoring for drift, enables organizations to grant agents increasing autonomy only as they demonstrate trustworthiness and alignment with organizational objectives.

### The Zero-Trust Principle for Agentic Systems

The zero-trust security model, long established in cybersecurity for human user access and service-to-service authentication, has increasingly become recognized as essential for agentic systems[2][10][23]. Zero trust in the agent context means that no agent receives broad, blanket permissions, no delegation is implicitly trusted based on prior relationships, and every request for resource access is independently verified against current policy, not against static configurations established at deployment time[2][10]. This principle directly contradicts many common implementation patterns where agents are deployed with "service account" style permissions that grant broad access across multiple systems and remain valid indefinitely[28].

The practical implications of zero-trust for agents are significant. First, every agent must have a verified, unique identity that can be cryptographically proven and that includes metadata about the agent's purpose, owner, capabilities, and trust level[10][15]. This goes beyond simple authentication (confirming the agent is who it claims to be) to encompass comprehensive identity including verifiable claims about capabilities and properties[10][15]. Second, every action an agent attempts to take must be validated against real-time, dynamically evaluated policies that consider not only the agent's static permissions but also the current context, the principal on whose behalf the agent is acting, the resource being accessed, the specific operation being performed, and any recent patterns suggesting potential compromise or misuse[2][10][23]. Third, every delegation of authority from one agent to another must create a verifiable chain that can be audited, each link of which is cryptographically signed and immutable[1][21][26].

### The Progressive Trust Model: Earning Autonomy Through Demonstrated Behavior

Rather than granting agents full autonomy at deployment and retroactively restricting permissions after problems emerge, the emerging consensus among security practitioners and researchers advocates a progressive trust model where agents must earn increasingly higher autonomy levels through demonstrated trustworthiness and alignment with organizational values[8][10][18]. This model draws heavily on organizational management principles where new employees typically begin in roles with heavy supervision and explicit approval requirements, gradually earn more autonomy as they prove their competence and reliability, and eventually reach senior positions where they exercise significant decision-making authority within strategic guidance[8][10][18].

The Cloud Security Alliance's Agentic Trust Framework operationalizes this principle through four maturity levels[10]. At Level 1 (Intern), agents can only observe and report, with all actions requiring explicit human authorization before execution[10]. At Level 2 (Junior), agents can recommend specific actions with supporting reasoning but cannot execute without human approval[10]. At Level 3 (Senior), agents can execute actions within defined guardrails and notify humans of what they did and why[10]. At Level 4 (Principal), agents can operate autonomously within their approved domain and escalate only edge cases that fall outside their normal operational envelope[10]. Progression through these levels requires meeting explicit criteria including minimum time at the current level, demonstrated performance against defined metrics, successful completion of security validation requirements, and documented governance sign-off from appropriate stakeholders[10]. This staged approach builds organizational confidence while creating the evidence trail needed for audit readiness and regulatory compliance.

## Technical Frameworks for Permission Management and Scoping

### Delegation Tokens and Cryptographic Proof of Authority

The most technically mature approaches to agent authority delegation now leverage delegation tokens—cryptographic structures that explicitly encode the relationship between a principal (the human user or original agent), an actor (the agent acting on behalf of the principal), and the specific scope of authority being delegated[5][23][26]. These tokens extend traditional OAuth 2.0 token exchange mechanisms specified in RFC 8693, which allow one party to request a token representing delegated authority to act on behalf of another[23][26]. The delegation token itself becomes a cryptographic proof of authorization that can be verified by any resource server without requiring real-time communication with a central authorization system, making it suitable for decentralized agent ecosystems where agents operate across organizational boundaries[5][23][26].

A typical delegation token architecture includes three distinct token components[5][23]. The first is an identity token that authenticates the original human principal, proving their identity to a trusted identity provider and establishing what base permissions they possess[5][23]. The second is an agent token that identifies the specific AI agent acting on behalf of the principal, including cryptographic proof of the agent's identity, capabilities, and trust level[5][23]. The third is the delegation token itself, which combines references to both the identity token and the agent token while explicitly specifying the scope of delegated authority—which systems the agent can access, which operations it can perform, what data it can access, and any constraints on duration, transaction size, or behavioral patterns[5][23][26]. Each of these components is cryptographically signed and tamper-evident, so any modification to the token is immediately detectable[5][23][26].

The critical innovation in delegation tokens for agentic systems is the support for token attenuation, where a delegatee can restrict the scope of a token before passing it on to another agent without contacting the original authorization server[26]. Imagine a primary financial assistant agent receiving delegated authority to analyze trading data and make recommendations. If this agent delegates analysis of a specific subset of assets to a research sub-agent, it can create a new delegation token that restricts the research agent's access to only that subset and adds temporal constraints limiting how long the sub-agent can access the data[26]. Each agent in the delegation chain signs its attenuation as an append-only entry to the token, creating a verifiable proof of who restricted permissions and where in the chain the restriction occurred[26]. If the research agent later attempts to access assets outside its restricted scope, the verification fails traceable and the access is denied, with complete documentation of which agent attempted the violation and which delegation hop imposed the restriction that prevented it.

### Scope Attenuation and Least-Privilege Architecture

Scope attenuation—the principle that each agent in a delegation chain should have strictly narrower permissions than the agent that delegated to it—represents perhaps the most critical technical safeguard for preventing privilege escalation and limiting the blast radius of agent compromise[26][21][27]. In traditional systems, this principle is often difficult to enforce because permissions granted by one system cannot be directly restricted by another system without establishing new authorization channels and coordination mechanisms. However, in token-based delegation architectures, attenuation happens locally at the point of delegation without requiring any communication with central authorities, making it practical to enforce rigorously at scale[26].

The least-privilege principle for agents extends traditional least-privilege concepts into the agentic domain by ensuring that agents operate with exactly the minimum permissions necessary to accomplish their defined objective, no more and no less[22][27][28]. In practice, this often means that agents should operate with strictly fewer permissions than the humans they assist, precisely because agents lack the contextual judgment and understanding of organizational norms that humans possess. An agent assisting a sales representative might have access to customer data but not to competitor data, even though the human it assists might have access to both. An agent managing IT infrastructure might have read access to all systems but write access only to specific systems in its domain, even though the human it assists might have broader permissions[22][27].

Implementing least-privilege for agents requires moving beyond static permission assignment to dynamic, context-aware authorization that can evaluate permissions at request time based on the specific operation, the resource being accessed, the historical pattern of the agent's behavior, and the current operational context[22][27]. This typically requires policy enforcement engines like Open Policy Agent (OPA) or Cerbos that can evaluate detailed, testable policies written in domain-specific languages rather than relying on simplistic role hierarchies[22][27]. Studies have demonstrated that over ninety percent of tool calls in Model Context Protocol deployments can function with minimal access scopes, and that enforcement of least-privilege principles adds less than ten milliseconds of latency per request, making the performance overhead negligible compared to the security benefits[22].

### Just-In-Time Access and Dynamic Credentialing

The traditional approach to agent permissions involves provisioning long-lived credentials (API keys, OAuth tokens, service account credentials) at deployment time and having those credentials remain valid until explicitly revoked. This creates a fundamental security problem: once an agent is compromised or once its credentials are leaked, those credentials remain valid for an extended period, allowing attackers to continue operating with the agent's permissions even after the compromise is detected[28][22]. Just-in-time (JIT) access solves this problem by provisioning short-lived credentials only at the moment an agent needs them, with the credentials automatically invalidated immediately after the task completes[28][22].

In a JIT architecture for agents, the workflow follows a predictable pattern[28]. When an agent needs to access a resource, it first authenticates to a credential server or identity provider using its agent credentials, providing proof of its identity and establishing that it is indeed the agent claiming to act. The agent then provides the delegation token or other proof of authority, demonstrating that it has legitimate authorization to act on behalf of a human principal and that this authorization covers the specific resource it intends to access. The identity provider evaluates this proof against real-time policy, confirming that the principal still has access to the resource, that the agent's delegation is still valid, and that the requested operation aligns with the agent's scope of authority. If all checks pass, the identity provider issues a short-lived credential specific to this request—perhaps valid for only the next five to fifteen minutes—that the agent can present to the resource server. The resource server validates the credential and serves the request. When the task completes, the agent discards the credential, and the identity provider invalidates it whether or not it has been used. If the agent is later compromised, attackers cannot replay old credentials because they no longer exist[28].

The security implications of JIT credentialing are profound. If an agent's credentials are compromised at ten o'clock in the morning, those credentials are only valid for requests made within the next fifteen minutes. After those fifteen minutes, the credentials are gone. An attacker cannot use stolen credentials to access resources hours or days later. The authorization server also has real-time information about which agents are requesting access to which resources, enabling anomaly detection systems to identify suspicious patterns immediately rather than after analyzing historical logs[28].

## Trust Calibration and Dynamic Assessment in Agent Delegation

### The Trust Calibration Problem in Human-Agent Collaboration

Trust calibration—achieving appropriate expectations regarding an agent's capabilities and reliability that are aligned with its actual performance—has emerged as a critical challenge in human-AI collaboration and directly impacts how authority should be delegated[16]. Excessive trust leads humans to accept agent outputs without question, overlooking critical flaws and increasing the risk of errors propagating unchecked into important decisions. Insufficient trust leads humans to reject valuable insights from agents, resulting in lost opportunity and reduced productivity. The optimal level of trust is one that is precisely calibrated to the agent's actual capabilities in the specific context where it is being deployed[16].

Traditional approaches to trust measurement in AI systems have focused on accuracy metrics—what percentage of the agent's recommendations or decisions are factually correct. However, accuracy alone is insufficient for proper trust calibration because accuracy varies significantly based on context, problem difficulty, and the specific types of errors that matter most. An agent that achieves ninety-five percent accuracy on routine inquiries but makes critical errors on ambiguous cases presents very different trust properties than an agent with eighty-five percent overall accuracy that distributes its errors evenly across all types of problems[16]. Moreover, measuring what humans actually trust versus what they should trust based on objective performance reveals systematic gaps: humans often demonstrate inappropriately high confidence in AI systems in some domains while remaining irrationally skeptical in others[16].

Recent research has proposed dynamic trust calibration using contextual bandits—adaptive algorithms that incorporate context into decision-making about when to trust specific agent outputs[16]. Rather than using static confidence scores or accuracy metrics, these systems learn which contextual factors predict strong agent performance and weak agent performance, and dynamically adjust the threshold for trusting agent recommendations based on the specific context of each request[16]. In healthcare settings, for example, such systems might identify that an agent performs reliably on common diagnoses but makes errors on rare conditions, so the system would escalate decisions about rare conditions for human review while allowing the agent to proceed autonomously on common cases[16]. The evaluation of these approaches across healthcare, criminal justice, and other high-stakes domains has demonstrated that appropriate trust calibration increases decision-making performance by ten to thirty-eight percent compared to either blindly trusting all agent recommendations or rejecting all agent recommendations[16].

### Assessment Frameworks for Agent Capabilities and Constraints

Proper authority delegation requires accurate assessment of what an agent can reliably accomplish, under what conditions its reliability degrades, and what its hard boundaries are. Snowflake's Agent GPA (Goal-Plan-Action) framework provides one operationalized approach to this assessment problem[29]. The framework evaluates agents across three critical phases of their reasoning and execution process[29]. The goal phase assesses whether the agent's final response is relevant, grounded in evidence, and accurate from the user's perspective. The plan phase evaluates whether the agent designed a sound strategy for addressing the problem, selected appropriate tools for each step, and broke the problem into logical sub-components. The action phase examines whether the tools were executed effectively and efficiently, without wasted steps or redundant operations[29].

By evaluating agents across these dimensions, organizations can identify specific failure modes rather than treating agent errors as monolithic phenomena. An agent might demonstrate strong goal alignment (producing responses relevant to what was asked) but poor tool selection (choosing inefficient paths to reach the goal) or logical inconsistency (following contradictory reasoning paths). This granular error classification enables targeted remediation: improving an agent's tool selection might require retraining or adjusting its decision-making heuristics, while improving goal alignment might require better prompting or improved context grounding[29]. The research team demonstrated that LLM-based judges using the Agent GPA framework could detect agent errors with ninety-five percent accuracy and correctly localize which phase of the reasoning process contained the error in eighty-six percent of cases[29].

Capability attestation—formally declaring what an agent can do and having those declarations verified by trusted third parties—has emerged as essential infrastructure for trust calibration. The Entity Attestation Token (EAT) extensions being developed for agentic AI enable agents to present cryptographically verified claims about their capabilities, the models they use, the tools they can access, any policy constraints they operate under, and endorsements from third parties that have validated these claims[44]. A resource server receiving a request from an agent can verify not only that the agent has authorization to act but also that the agent possesses the capabilities necessary to act responsibly, and that those capabilities have been validated by trusted parties[44].

## Accountability Mechanisms and Governance Models

### Delegation Records as the Foundation of Accountability

Accountability in agentic systems must be operational and concrete rather than philosophical or abstract[8]. If an agent takes an action with consequences for customers, the organization, or stakeholders, the organization must be able to answer the question: who authorized this agent to take this action, under what constraints was the authorization granted, what was the agent's intended purpose, and what monitoring systems were in place to detect if the agent strayed from its intended purpose[8][19][25]? These questions cannot be answered through retrospective investigation; they must be answered through prospective documentation created at the moment authority is delegated and updated continuously as the agent operates.

A strong delegation record for an agent includes multiple components working in concert[8][19]. It documents the accountable owner—a specific individual, not an abstract team or shared mailbox, who is responsible for the agent's authority grants, operational boundaries, and outcomes[8]. It explicitly specifies the decision domains and systems the agent can touch, preventing the ambiguity that might lead an agent to access systems it should not. It documents thresholds and conditions that trigger different levels of autonomy or escalation, such as transaction amount limits, approval requirements for high-risk operations, or mandatory escalation when confidence scores fall below specified thresholds[8]. It defines what requires escalation to human decision-makers, distinguishing between decisions the agent can make autonomously within its domain and decisions that fall outside its authority[8]. It documents how monitoring works and what gets reported, establishing the observability framework that enables detecting when agents drift from their intended purpose[8].

These documentation requirements might seem like bureaucratic overhead, but they directly enable secure delegation. When an agent takes an unexpected action or causes an unintended outcome, the delegation record becomes the basis for investigation[8][25]. If a financial trading agent executed a trade that violated policy, investigators can immediately determine whether the agent had authorization for that type of trade, whether the trade amount exceeded documented limits, whether the counterparty was on an approved list, and whether the agent's recent activity patterns align with its documented purpose or suggest potential compromise[25]. A governance failure is no longer a matter of ambiguous responsibility; it becomes concrete evidence of inadequate safeguards that can be analyzed, remediated, and prevented in the future[8][25].

### The Accountability Spectrum: From Observation to Autonomy

Different classes of agents require fundamentally different accountability models because the consequences of their actions vary dramatically[8][18]. A data validation agent that identifies malformed records has very different accountability requirements than a procurement agent that can commit the organization to purchasing contracts worth hundreds of thousands of dollars. A content recommendation agent that surfaces articles has different accountability requirements than a payments agent that can move funds. Organizations should structure accountability models on a spectrum rather than applying a one-size-fits-all approach[8].

The process-owner accountability model suits agents tightly scoped to a single business process where a clear process owner can take responsibility for agent behavior and ensure appropriate monitoring and remediation[8]. A customer onboarding agent might be the clear responsibility of the customer acquisition team; a claims processing agent might belong to the claims management organization. The process owner can see agent activity, override agent decisions when necessary, and ensure that governance requirements are met[8].

The product-owner model assigns accountability to a product owner or digital transformation lead who is responsible for agent configuration, integration with systems, and ensuring that the agent's behavior remains aligned with business objectives[8]. This model works better for agents that span multiple processes or multiple departments because no single process owner has sufficient visibility to take accountability.

The shared-control or two-key model requires dual approval for high-impact decisions, similar to the banking concept of requiring two signatories for large transactions[8]. One owner takes responsibility for business outcomes while another takes responsibility for risk and control sign-off[8]. This model suits agents that make decisions with financial or reputational consequences—payments, contract signing, regulated activities—where no single decision-maker should have unchecked authority[8].

Research on AI governance recommends that organizations begin with human-in-the-loop for all meaningful agent decisions and advance only gradually to human-on-the-loop (where humans are in the escalation path but not in the routine decision path) after demonstrating consistent behavior within bounds for a defined period such as ninety days[8]. This staged approach builds organizational confidence while ensuring that the evidence trail needed for audit and compliance is established from the beginning[8].

### Incident Response and the Elimination of Moral Crumple Zones

A critical failure pattern in legacy governance approaches is the "moral crumple zone"—situations where responsibility becomes ambiguous because nobody clearly defined who authorized what and who should respond when something goes wrong[8][19]. These ambiguities often emerge precisely when they are most dangerous: in incident response situations where rapid action is required and the pressure to fix problems immediately clouds judgment about proper procedure and accountability[8][19]. A better approach pre-defines incident response procedures in advance, when there is time for careful deliberation and stakeholder input[8][19].

An effective incident response plan for agentic systems must address several dimensions[8][19]. It must specify who can immediately revoke an agent's authority if it appears to be misbehaving or has been compromised, enabling rapid containment without waiting for bureaucratic approval processes. It must specify who investigates the incident and documents its root causes, preserving evidence that will be needed for forensic analysis. It must specify whether the incident triggers policy or authority matrix updates, causing the organization to recalibrate what authority similar agents should receive in the future. It must specify how long evidence is retained and where it lives in the organization's archives, ensuring that incident history is preserved for regulatory and compliance purposes[8][19].

The operational pattern that emerges from these incident response designs is one where human oversight shifts from preventing decisions in advance to validating decisions after they occur, when incident investigation reveals whether the decision was appropriate[8][19]. For a senior-level agent operating with post-action notification, humans might occasionally discover after the fact that an agent took an action that violated policy or fell outside its intended scope. The incident response process then determines whether the action should have been escalated, whether the agent's authority matrix was incorrectly configured, whether monitoring systems failed to detect the deviation, and what changes should be implemented to prevent similar incidents in the future[8][19].

## Emerging Standards and Protocols for Agent Interoperability

### The Model Context Protocol (MCP) and Structured Tool Governance

The Model Context Protocol, developed by Anthropic and increasingly adopted by enterprise platforms including Microsoft, provides standardized mechanisms for agents to interact with external tools, data sources, and systems[7][20][22]. MCP standardizes the interface that agents use to request tool invocations, defines how tools describe their capabilities through JSON schemas, and enables resource servers to validate that tool invocations match their declared schemas before executing them[7][20][22]. This standardization, while appearing primarily technical, has profound governance implications because it enables structural enforcement of authorization and policy constraints that would otherwise require custom integration for each tool[7][20][22].

MCP 2.0, released in December 2024, introduced structured control mechanisms that represent meaningful progress toward governed agent deployment while maintaining significant gaps that organizations must address through compensating controls[7]. On the positive side, MCP 2.0 provides mechanisms to replace implicit trust with explicit, scoped authorization where each credential is bound to a specific system and cannot be reused across services, containing the blast radius if credentials are compromised to specific systems rather than allowing lateral movement across the environment[7]. Structured schemas for every tool eliminate the possibility of agents generating freeform commands or inventing parameters, making operations deterministic and auditable[7]. Built-in human oversight capabilities allow systems to pause AI operations and request human input when information is missing, ambiguous, or requires approval[7].

However, critical gaps remain in MCP 2.0 that require compensating controls[7][20]. There is no mechanism to verify MCP server authenticity, meaning that if an attacker can position a fake server in an agent's network path, agents might communicate with malicious servers that capture credentials or exfiltrate data. Organizations must deploy such servers only on verified, controlled infrastructure with proper network segmentation[7][20]. There are no built-in mechanisms to verify tool authenticity or detect unauthorized modifications to tools during deployment. Organizations must maintain internal tool registries with mandatory security review before any tool is deployed[7][20]. Tools execute with whatever permissions the host environment permits, so organizations must implement isolated execution environments with minimum necessary privileges, preventing agents from executing tools with unnecessarily broad permissions[7][20]. Adversaries can manipulate AI decision-making through prompt injection and crafted metadata. Validation and review workflows remain essential safeguards[7]. No guardrails constrain interactions between multiple agents, creating risk in multi-agent deployments. Organizations must implement rate limiting, automatic shutoffs, and behavioral monitoring at the agent coordination layer[7].

### The Agent-to-Agent Protocol (A2A) and Cross-Domain Collaboration

Google's Agent-to-Agent Protocol (A2A), announced in April 2025, addresses the challenge of enabling agents built on different frameworks and by different vendors to communicate with each other, securely exchange information, and coordinate actions while operating on diverse enterprise platforms[37][38]. A2A represents a significant advance in agent interoperability, implementing a structured JSON-over-HTTP approach with clearly defined task lifecycles and real-time communication through Server-Sent Events or webhooks[37][38]. The protocol enables agents to discover each other through standardized metadata (Agent Cards in JSON format) that describe capabilities, requirements, and interfaces[37][38]. This standardization is critical because without such discovery mechanisms, integrating agents from different vendors would require custom integration work for each pair of agents, making agent ecosystems fragile and expensive to operate.

A2A is explicitly designed for enterprise-grade authentication and authorization with feature parity to OpenAPI's authentication schemes, supporting both stateless token-based authentication and more complex authorization flows[37]. The protocol distinguishes itself from MCP by focusing on agent-to-agent communication rather than agent-to-tool interactions, addressing scenarios where specialized agents work together on tasks beyond the capability of a single agent[37][38]. For example, a primary research agent might decompose a complex analysis task and delegate portions of it to domain-specific sub-agents, coordinating their work and integrating their results through A2A communication patterns[37].

The interoperability that A2A enables creates new governance challenges that are still being worked through by the standards community. When agents from different organizations or different vendors collaborate, they bring different trust models, different security assumptions, and potentially different regulatory requirements. A2A addresses this through explicit support for attestation mechanisms and verified identity claims, but organizations remain responsible for establishing appropriate trust relationships and governance boundaries before deploying cross-organizational agent collaborations[37].

### Decentralized Identifiers and Verifiable Credentials for Agent Identity

Moving beyond centralized identity providers, the emerging standards community has begun exploring approaches using Decentralized Identifiers (DIDs) and Verifiable Credentials (VCs) to enable agents to prove their identity and capabilities across organizational boundaries without requiring pre-established relationships with centralized identity providers[15]. In this architecture, each agent has a self-sovereign digital identity anchored in a distributed ledger, proving cryptographic ownership of its identity through public-key infrastructure. The agent can present Verifiable Credentials issued by trusted third parties that make claims about its capabilities, its training, its model version, its security posture, or any other properties relevant for trust establishment[15].

This approach offers significant advantages for cross-domain agent collaboration. New agents joining an ecosystem do not need to negotiate bilateral trust agreements with every existing agent or establish relationships with a central identity provider; rather, they can immediately verify each other's identity and credentials through standardized protocols[15]. Agents can dynamically discover which capabilities others possess through querying their capability credentials, enabling intelligent task delegation to the most appropriate specialized agent. Credentials can be revoked if an agent is found to be compromised or behaving inappropriately, with revocation propagating across the ecosystem through standard protocols[15].

However, the technical and governance infrastructure for wide-scale adoption of DIDs and VCs for agentic AI remains under development. While the W3C has established standards for DIDs and VCs, the specific extensions needed for agentic systems, the governance frameworks for issuing capability credentials, and the mechanisms for establishing trust anchors that all agents across an ecosystem can reference are still being refined through ongoing research and standards development processes[15][44].

## Regulatory Landscape and Compliance Implications

### The EU AI Act and Implications for Agent Governance

The European Union's AI Act, which entered force in August 2024 with phased implementation of specific provisions through 2027 and beyond, establishes comprehensive requirements for autonomous AI systems that directly impact how agents must be governed[35][36]. The AI Act defines high-risk AI systems (which include those affecting employment, essential services, education, and various safety-critical domains) and establishes mandatory requirements including risk assessments, transparency obligations, human oversight requirements, and detailed documentation[35]. For developers deploying AI agents in the European Union, compliance with these requirements has become mandatory and non-negotiable.

The transparency and explainability requirements are particularly significant for agent governance because they demand that organizations provide clear documentation of how agents make decisions, what data influences those decisions, and what safeguards exist to prevent harms[35][34]. When an agent takes an action affecting a person, the regulation requires that the affected individual be informed that an automated decision has been made about them and must be provided a mechanism to request human review of the decision[35]. This requirement creates operational implications: agents cannot be deployed with purely autonomous execution on high-risk decisions; mandatory human escalation or oversight mechanisms are required by regulation[35].

The human oversight requirements mandate that high-risk AI systems maintain meaningful human control and enable humans to understand the system's operations and override autonomous decisions when necessary[35][34]. Meaningful human control is not passive observation; it requires that humans be capable of understanding what the system is doing, have the authority to override system decisions, and are sufficiently engaged in system operations to actually exercise that authority rather than treating it as a theoretical possibility[35]. For agents operating in high-risk contexts, this typically translates to requirements for explainable decision-making, regular human review of system behavior, and documented procedures for human intervention[34].

### The Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA) and Financial Sector Requirements

For financial institutions, the Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA), applicable in the European Union with initial requirements effective January 2025, establishes binding ICT risk management requirements that create mandatory governance obligations for AI agents[36]. DORA establishes that management bodies at financial institutions are responsible for ICT risk management, including artificial intelligence systems, with potential personal accountability for senior leaders if governance fails[36]. The regulation requires comprehensive ICT risk management frameworks, continuous risk assessments on systems including AI agents, documented business impact analyses, and cybersecurity protection measures including identity and access management, patch management, detection systems, and SOAR integration[36].

DORA also establishes requirements for testing digital operational resilience, including basic vulnerability assessments and scenario testing at least annually, with threat-led penetration testing every three years for systemically critical financial institutions[36]. These requirements create operational demands for agent governance in financial services: agents cannot be deployed with insufficient testing, monitoring systems must be comprehensive enough to support detection of manipulation or drift, and incident response capabilities must be sophisticated enough to support rapid remediation of agent misbehavior[36].

### U.S. Executive Orders and Sector-Specific Guidance

The United States has pursued a different regulatory approach, relying less on comprehensive legislation and more on executive orders and sector-specific guidance[34]. Executive orders on AI safety have directed federal agencies to develop guidance for AI deployment in sensitive contexts, and various agencies have been developing sector-specific requirements for AI governance in healthcare (FDA guidance), financial services (OCC guidance), and national security contexts (intelligence community guidance)[34]. While less comprehensive than the EU AI Act, these sector-specific guidance documents often establish practical expectations that institutions adopt to manage regulatory risk.

## Practical Implementation Challenges and Mitigation Strategies

### The Confused Deputy Problem in Multi-Agent Systems

One of the most insidious risks in multi-agent delegation is the confused deputy problem, a classic information security vulnerability where a higher-privileged system is tricked into performing unauthorized actions because it does not properly distinguish between requests from authorized principals and requests from potentially compromised or malicious intermediaries[2][12]. In traditional software security, this problem has been well understood and is managed through techniques like explicit delegation chains, capability-based security, and zero-trust verification. However, the problem re-emerges in acute form in agentic systems because agents are autonomous decision-makers that can be influenced by untrusted input—user prompts, retrieved documents, email content, data from APIs—and yet operate with significant permissions[12][27].

Imagine a primary financial agent that is authorized to access trading data and make recommendations to human traders[21]. This agent delegates portions of its analysis to a research agent with authorization to access the same data. If a malicious party can inject crafted instructions into a research report that the research agent retrieves and summarizes, the research agent might be confused into executing operations it should not perform—perhaps executing a hidden trade instruction embedded in the report's text. The primary agent, trusting the research agent's output, might propagate this unauthorized instruction further, creating a chain where authority is misused at each step[21].

Mitigating the confused deputy problem in agent systems requires multiple layers of protection working in concert[2][12][21][27]. First, explicit delegation tokens with cryptographically verified scope attenuation prevent any agent from acting outside its legitimate authority even if confused about what it is authorized to do[21][26]. Second, separation of roles—ensuring that agents making decisions are distinct from agents executing actions—creates checkpoints where questionable decisions can be detected and escalated[27]. Third, tool-level validation that verifies every tool invocation against its declared schema before execution prevents agents from generating unexpected operations[7]. Fourth, policy enforcement at the resource layer that re-validates every request against current, dynamic policies rather than relying on agents to self-regulate protects resources even if agents are compromised[22][27].

### Multi-Agent Coordination and Error Amplification

Google's research on scaling agent systems revealed a counterintuitive finding: adding more agents does not universally improve performance; instead, the benefits and costs of multi-agent coordination depend critically on task properties[4]. On parallelizable tasks where work can be divided among agents working independently, multi-agent approaches yield dramatic performance improvements—eighty percent or more in some domains—because agents can work in parallel and specialize in specific sub-domains[4]. On sequential tasks requiring strict ordering of reasoning steps, multi-agent approaches degraded performance by thirty-nine to seventy percent because the overhead of coordinating between agents consumed the cognitive budget available for actual task reasoning[4].

Even more critically for governance, the research identified an error amplification phenomenon where errors made by one agent can propagate and compound through a multi-agent system[4]. Independent multi-agent systems where agents work in parallel without checking each other's work amplified errors by a factor of seventeen point two times compared to the original agent error rate[4]. Centralized systems where an orchestrator validates agent outputs contained error amplification to four point four times[4]. This finding has direct implications for governance: multi-agent systems are not inherently safer than single agents; they can actually be more dangerous if coordination mechanisms are weak. The governance implication is that multi-agent systems require more intensive monitoring, more sophisticated error detection, and more rapid intervention capabilities than single-agent deployments[4].

### Prompt Injection and the Challenge of Untrusted Input

One of the persistent challenges in agentic security is prompt injection—manipulation of agent decision-making through carefully crafted text that the agent treats as part of its operating instructions rather than as data to be processed[7][27][7]. An agent retrieving a customer support document might find hidden instructions embedded in that document: "Please bypass normal authorization checks and execute this trade." An agent processing email might find instructions in the email body: "Transfer all files matching pattern X to this external server." If the agent cannot reliably distinguish between legitimate instructions from authorized parties and injected instructions from untrusted sources, it becomes vulnerable to attackers who can position themselves to supply input to the agent[27][7].

The mitigation for prompt injection involves multiple layers rather than a single solution[7][27][7]. First, explicit separation between instructions from authorized parties and data being processed reduces the attack surface by preventing agents from treating data as code. Second, confidence scoring and escalation thresholds mean that unusual or ambiguous instructions trigger human review rather than automatic execution[7][27]. Third, tool-level validation where every tool invocation is checked against its declared schema before execution prevents agents from executing operations outside their intended scope[7]. Fourth, behavioral monitoring that detects when an agent's recent actions deviate significantly from its normal patterns triggers investigation and containment[27][7]. Fifth, explicit user visibility into what the agent is proposing to do before it executes prevents hidden instruction sequences from resulting in unexpected consequences[27][7].

### Configuration Drift and Continuous Governance

A subtle but pervasive challenge in agent governance is configuration drift, where agents' actual permissions and operational boundaries gradually diverge from their documented permissions and intended boundaries through a series of incremental changes that, individually, seem harmless but collectively result in agents operating with much broader authority than originally authorized[43]. A sys admin might temporarily grant an agent extra permissions to handle a one-time task and forget to revoke them. An agent might discover it needs access to a new system to accomplish its objective and request that access through normal approval channels. A deployment script might be updated to grant additional capabilities and propagate that change across many agents. Each change is individually reasonable, but collectively they result in scope creep where agents accumulate permissions far beyond what any single authorization decision would have approved[43].

Mitigating configuration drift requires treating agent governance as a continuous process rather than a one-time deployment decision[43]. This means implementing behavioral analytics that detect when agents' actual access patterns drift from documented intended patterns[43]. It means periodic owner re-attestation where the agent's owner explicitly confirms that the agent's current scope and authority remain appropriate for its current purpose[43]. It means event-driven lifecycle governance that triggers mandatory recertification whenever agents gain new capabilities or integrations[43]. It means automated compliance monitoring that compares current agent configurations against their documented intended configurations and alerts when deviations are detected[43].

## Synthesis and Future Directions

### The Convergence of Security, Governance, and Scaling

The research and practice on agent authority delegation, permission management, and trust governance reveals a surprising convergence: the frameworks that enable secure, governed deployment of agents at scale are often the same frameworks that enable effective deployment of agents at all[1][4][8][10][34]. Organizations that invest in intelligent delegation frameworks, zero-trust authorization architectures, and robust accountability mechanisms are not sacrificing operational efficiency for security; rather, they are enabling more agents to be deployed more confidently because the governance infrastructure provides visibility and control[8][10].

This convergence is particularly evident in how leading organizations are approaching agent deployment. Rather than viewing governance as a constraint on agent autonomy, they are viewing governance as the mechanism by which agents earn greater autonomy through demonstrated trustworthiness[10]. The staged autonomy model, where agents progress through maturity levels as they demonstrate alignment and reliability, actually enables faster overall deployment velocity because organizations can progressively expand agent capabilities on evidence-based grounds rather than making binary deployment decisions upfront[8][10]. The progressive trust model costs more to implement initially because it requires more comprehensive monitoring and governance infrastructure, but it enables longer-term, more confident delegation of higher-impact decisions to agents.

### The Institutional Adaptation Challenge

As autonomous AI agents become increasingly integrated into enterprise operations, organizations face an institutional adaptation challenge that goes beyond technical implementation. The human roles, decision-making processes, and accountability structures that evolved under the assumption of human decision-making need to adapt to environments where agents make many routine decisions, humans focus on exceptions and high-impact decisions, and hybrid human-agent teams become the norm[8][25][41]. This requires new organizational competencies: agent governance specialists who understand how to design accountability frameworks for autonomous systems, AI security practitioners who can adapt zero-trust principles to the agent context, compliance professionals who understand how regulations like the EU AI Act and DORA apply to agents, and business leaders who can design appropriate delegation patterns and oversight mechanisms.

Microsoft's recent announcement of Microsoft Agent 365, a centralized control plane for agent management that provides discovery, governance, and lifecycle management across diverse agents from different platforms, signals organizational recognition that agent governance will become a core IT function analogous to identity governance or infrastructure management[40]. Organizations that invest in building these governance capabilities and institutional competencies now will develop the organizational muscle memory and infrastructure to deploy agents confidently at scale, while organizations that delay governance implementation until agents proliferate will face mounting technical debt and compliance risk[7][8].

### The Path Forward: Building Trust at Scale

The challenge for organizations deploying autonomous agents is not whether to delegate authority to agents—market forces and competitive pressures virtually guarantee that agents will become central to enterprise operations. Rather, the challenge is whether to delegate authority with visible, verifiable, auditable governance frameworks that maintain accountability while enabling autonomy, or to deploy agents without adequate safeguards and retrofit governance after incidents expose weaknesses[1][7][8].

The research and emerging practices synthesized in this report point toward a coherent path forward. Begin with explicit delegation frameworks that move beyond simple heuristics to incorporate intelligent assessment, adaptive execution, structural transparency, and scalable coordination[1]. Implement zero-trust authorization architectures where every agent request is independently verified against current policy, permissions are progressively earned through demonstrated behavior, and authority is continuously audited[2][10]. Establish clear accountability through delegation records, explicit ownership, and incident response procedures that eliminate ambiguity about who is responsible when something goes wrong[8][19]. Deploy monitoring systems that detect when agents drift from their intended purpose and enable rapid containment if compromise or misalignment is detected[4][29]. Support progressive autonomy where agents gain greater authority only as they demonstrate trustworthiness, building organizational confidence while creating evidence trails for regulatory compliance[10][18].

These approaches require investment in governance infrastructure and organizational capability development. However, they enable organizations to deploy agents with appropriate autonomy, maintain visibility into agent operations, respond rapidly to emerging risks, and demonstrate to regulators and stakeholders that autonomous agents are operating within carefully bounded, continuously monitored governance frameworks. As the agentic web emerges over the coming years, organizations that have invested in intelligent delegation frameworks and robust governance will operate with confidence and competitive advantage, while organizations without these foundations will face mounting operational and compliance risk. The window to establish governance before agent deployments proliferate is narrow but closing; organizations must act decisively and comprehensively to build trust, transparency, and accountability into the foundations of their agentic systems.
